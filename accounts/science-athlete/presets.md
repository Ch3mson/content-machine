# Science Athlete Presets

Use this file to choose repeatable copy structures across sports.

## Preset Index

| Preset | Use for | Hook behavior | Product fit |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Skill Transfer List | Common training mistakes and practical fixes | "how to get more fluid..." or "why you feel stiff..." | Late slide if mental reps fit |
| Read System | Aspirational breakdown of elite skill | "the 4 reads behind..." | Optional, usually close only |
| Drill Upgrade | Replacing a clean drill with a more game-like version | "the drills that do not transfer..." | Rare unless visualization extends correction |
| Pressure State | Why skill disappears under pressure | "why you freeze..." | Strong fit for visualization |

## 1) Skill Transfer List

### Use When

- Athlete feels good in drills but stiff in games.
- The sport has common drills that remove the key game cue.
- The post should feel like a clean educational list.

### Structure

1. Hero — name the felt problem.
2. Item 1 — visual information or scanning gap.
3. Item 2 — body orientation, rhythm, or timing gap.
4. Item 3 — drill design gap.
5. Item 4 — pressure, fatigue, or mental rehearsal gap.
6. Close — summarize the system.

### Example Hooks

- `how to get more fluid on the ball:`
- `why your handle disappears in games:`
- `why you freeze in sparring:`
- `why your drill skill does not transfer:`

## 2) Read System

### Use When

- The user wants a different angle from "what to avoid."
- The sport skill depends on reading defenders, cues, space, or rhythm.
- The tone should feel aspirational and smart.

### Structure

1. Hero — `the 4 reads behind elite [skill]`
2. Read 1 — primary body/space cue.
3. Read 2 — secondary pressure cue.
4. Read 3 — second defender/layer/context cue.
5. Read 4 — self-regulation cue, tempo, breathing, or rhythm.
6. Close — skill is not a move collection; it is a read system.

### Example Hooks

- `the 4 reads behind elite handles:`
- `the 4 reads behind calm midfielders:`
- `the 4 reads behind clean counters:`

## 3) Drill Upgrade

### Use When

- The post compares a familiar drill against a better version.
- The value comes from practical training implementation.
- Diagrams are central to the post.

### Structure

1. Hero — name the drill that does not transfer.
2. Drill flaw — what cue the drill removes.
3. Easy upgrade — accessible setup.
4. Why it works — simple science explanation.
5. Progression — harder version or mental rehearsal.
6. Close — better drills give the brain better information.

### Example Fixes

- Linear cones → scattered cones.
- Memorized combo → random gate cue.
- Same-speed dribble → slow-hold-burst rhythm.
- Square receive → wall pass plus exit gate.
- Shadow slipping → slip line with random visual cue.

## 4) Pressure State

### Use When

- The athlete performs well alone but freezes under pressure.
- The post explains threat response, fatigue, crowd noise, consequence, or fear of mistakes.
- w(inner) can naturally appear as pressure-picture rehearsal.

### Structure

1. Hero — name the pressure failure.
2. Clean environment — why the skill works alone.
3. Pressure trigger — what changes in the nervous system.
4. Practical pressure drill — easy controlled stressor.
5. Mental reps — rehearse the exact pressure picture.
6. Close — pressure feels less new when the brain has already seen it.

## Selection Rules

- Use **Skill Transfer List** for broad educational posts.
- Use **Read System** when the user asks for a fresh angle.
- Use **Drill Upgrade** when training diagrams are the main value.
- Use **Pressure State** when the product bridge is important.
