- Fear of messing up in front of teammates and coach
- Self-doubt: "Why do I disappear when it counts?"
- Identity gap between practice self and game self
- Shame about being "conservative" in games when they know they're capable of more
- Pressure makes them "tense" and revert to safe choices
- Coaches may see them as training-only players

## Mechanisms That May Explain The Pain
1. **Representative Learning Design gap**: Training often removes the exact information sources the game provides (defender cues, timing pressure, space reads, consequence). Travassos et al. (2012): "Training transfers better when it preserves the information the game gives you."
2. **Perception-action coupling disruption**: Under pressure, the brain defaults to what's automatic, not what's recently learned. The perception-action loop [the link between what you see and how your body responds] slows down.
3. **Threat response / amygdala activation**: Pressure triggers the brain's threat-detection system. Body tightens. Decision options shrink. Player chooses lowest-risk action before consciously realizing it.
4. **Contextual interference**: Blocked/repetitive practice feels good but produces poor transfer. Variable, random practice feels messier but builds adaptability (Shea & Morgan, 1979).
5. **Mental imagery functional equivalence**: Imagined movements activate shared brain areas as physical movements (Jeannerod, 1994; Holmes & Collins, 2001). PETTLEP imagery can support skill retention and confidence when physical practice lacks game-specific information.

## Failed Solutions Observed
- "Just play for fun" — explicitly tried and failed (BigSoccer: "Tried to get him to just play for fun, but that doesn't seem to work either.")
- More solo/individual training with a private trainer — often makes the gap worse because it removes pressure and opposition
- More 1v1 reps alone — stays in "comfort of solo training," doesn't add game context
- Generic "be confident" advice — doesn't address the mechanism
- Small-sided games alone — helpful but still may not preserve the specific information the game gives a winger

## Different Solutions That Work
- **Representative task design**: Keep key perception-action cues from competition (Pinder et al., 2011). Add random cues, defender decisions, and consequence to training.
- **Variable/random practice**: Change practice order and constraints so the body has to read before acting (Shea & Morgan, 1979).
- **PETTLEP mental imagery**: Game-specific visualization that includes Physical, Environment, Task, Timing, Learning, Emotion, and Perspective — especially emotion and timing, which are the cues missing from physical training.
- **Pressure-picture rehearsal**: Mental reps of the exact 1v1 scenario with game emotion, so the brain has already processed the threat response before the real match.

## Claim-Risk Notes
- Pressure/threat response claims are HIGH risk per sources.md. Frame as "pressure can shrink perceived options" not "your amygdala makes you choke."
- Mental imagery claims are MEDIUM risk. Safe when framed as "can support performance, especially when paired with physical practice" (Driskell et al., 1994).
- Do not claim w(inner) fixes anxiety or guarantees transfer.
- Use "linked with" / "can support" language for research claims.
