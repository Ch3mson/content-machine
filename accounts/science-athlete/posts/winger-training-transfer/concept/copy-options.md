# Copy Options: Flow B — The Betrayal, The Proof, The Rebuild

## Variation A: Direct and Sharp

### Slide 1 — Hero
**Role:** Provocation / hook

You beat everyone in training.
Then the game starts.
You disappear.

Your 1v1 reps are making you a worse winger.

**Image:** Aesthetic winger image, B&W or desaturated, bold stacked title
**Notes:** Manual line breaks after each sentence for impact.

---

### Slide 2 — The Proof
**Role:** Research + stat for identification

You train four days a week. You look skilled. You feel fast.

Research on representative learning design found that training transfers better when it preserves the information the game gives you. Travassos et al. (2012): practice that keeps key game cues produces real match performance. Your drills removed defender reads, timing pressure, and consequence.

**the fix:** Count how many reps include a live read or random cue. If the number is low, your practice is built for highlights, not games.

**Source:** Travassos et al. (2012)
**Notes:** ~75 words. One science term: representative learning design (explained in context).
**Claim-risk:** Safe framing. No clinical claims.

---

### Slide 3 — The Betrayal
**Role:** Blocked vs variable practice

You learned the step-over. You learned the double touch.
You did not learn when to use them.

Blocked practice repeats one pattern. Variable practice changes the cue every rep. Shea & Morgan (1979): changing practice order improves transfer even when it feels messier.

Your clean reps created a false version of skill.

**the fix:** Replace one blocked drill with variable reps. Change starting positions. Add a random defender cue. Make the read decide the move.

**Source:** Shea & Morgan (1979)
**Notes:** ~68 words. No unfamiliar science terms.
**Claim-risk:** Safe historical reference.

---

### Slide 4 — Physical Rebuild
**Role:** Practical training correction

Your body knows the move. It does not know the moment.

Perception-action coupling [the link between what you see and how your body responds] needs game information. Without it, the body guesses.

**the fix:** Set up a 1v1 drill with random cues. One cone signals go right. Another signals go left. The defender decides. You read, then move. No memorized routes.

**Source:** Travassos et al. (2012)
**Notes:** ~62 words. One bracketed term.
**Claim-risk:** Medium. Explain simply.

---

### Slide 5 — Mental Rebuild
**Role:** Mental imagery fix

You cannot add live defenders to every session.
Your brain still needs the game picture.

Mental imagery activates the same brain areas as physical movement. Holmes & Collins (2001): the PETTLEP model says imagery works best when it matches the real task. Same position. Same emotion. Same timing.

**the fix:** Before bed, replay one game 1v1 in first-person. Feel the pressure. See the defender's hips. Decide. Execute. Repeat three times.

**Source:** Holmes & Collins (2001)
**Notes:** ~68 words. One unfamiliar term: PETTLEP [explained as matching the real task].
**Claim-risk:** Medium. "Activates same brain areas" is supported by functional equivalence research.
