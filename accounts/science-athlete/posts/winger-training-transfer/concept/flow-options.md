# Flow Architecture Options

## Flow A: The Lie, The Mechanism, The Fix, The Bridge

**Concept name:** Two Versions of You

**Clickbait hero direction:**
"You beat everyone in training. Then the game starts and you disappear. Here's why your practice is lying to you."

**Slide count range:** 5-7 slides

**Sequential slide jobs:**
1. **Hero:** Name the gap. You look dangerous in 1v1s. You look forgettable in games. (Hook: why?)
2. **The lie:** Training removes the exact information the game gives you. No defender reads. No timing pressure. No consequence. You learned the move, not the moment.
3. **The mechanism:** Under pressure, your brain defaults to what's automatic. If your automatic was learned without game cues, it doesn't transfer. Representative learning design — keep the game's information in practice.
4. **Physical fix:** Stop blocked reps. Start variable practice. Random exits. Changing cues. One read before every receive. The drill must look more like the game, not less.
5. **Mental fix:** When your body can't train under match pressure, your brain still can. Mental imagery with game-specific emotion and timing builds the automatic response you're missing.
6. **Product bridge (optional):** w(inner) generates personalized visualization audio for your sport, position, and scenario — mental reps when physical practice can't replicate the game.
7. **Close:** Your training self is real. But only if you train the information the game actually gives you.

**Curiosity engine:**
Each slide answers a question the reader already has: "Why do I disappear?" → "Because training removed the cues." → "So what do I do?" → "Add randomness and read cues." → "What about when I can't?" → "Mental reps."

**Why this structure fits the selected angle:**
It mirrors the reader's internal monologue: confusion → realization → action → tool. The product bridge comes only after the physical fix is established.

**Image implications:**
- Hero: aesthetic athlete image, B&W or desaturated
- Slide 2: contrast diagram — clean training grid vs chaotic game scenario
- Slide 3: simple attention/diagram showing information removal
- Slide 4: top-down whiteboard drill diagram with random cues
- Slide 5: athlete in quiet focus, headphones, mental rehearsal
- Slide 6: subtle app context or athlete-with-phone visual
- Slide 7: bold statement card or athlete image

**Claim-risk notes:**
- Keep imagery claims modest: "can support performance" not "guarantees transfer."
- Frame pressure response as information loss, not brain failure.

## Flow B: The Betrayal, The Proof, The Rebuild

**Concept name:** Your Training Is Making You Worse at Games

**Clickbait hero direction:**
"Your 1v1 reps are making you a worse winger. Not better. Here's the science."

**Slide count range:** 5-7 slides

**Sequential slide jobs:**
1. **Hero:** Provocation. The more you drill 1v1s alone, the worse you get at real 1v1s. (Shock + self-recognition)
2. **The proof:** Research on representative learning design. Travassos et al.: training transfers better when it preserves game information. Your blocked reps removed the exact cues you need.
3. **The betrayal:** You trained a pattern. The game demands adaptation. Blocked practice feels good. Variable practice feels messy. But variable practice is what transfers.
4. **The rebuild — physical:** How to redesign your 1v1 reps. Add defender decisions. Change starting positions. Require a read before every move. Make the drill unpredictable.
5. **The rebuild — mental:** When you can't add a live defender, add a mental one. Game-picture imagery with emotion and timing. PETTLEP model: physical, environment, task, timing, learning, emotion, perspective.
6. **Product bridge (optional):** w(inner) builds personalized visualization audio for your exact position and scenario — so your mental defender behaves like your real one.
7. **Close:** Stop collecting moves. Start collecting reads. The game doesn't reward clean technique. It rewards correct decisions under uncertainty.

**Curiosity engine:**
The provocation creates disagreement. The proof resolves it with science. The rebuild gives them a system. The close reframes what "good" means.

**Why this structure fits the selected angle:**
It uses confrontation ("your training is making you worse") which matches the science-athlete slightly confrontational tone. It gives the reader a reason to feel angry at their current training, then channels that into action.

**Image implications:**
- Hero: bold provocative statement over athlete image
- Slide 2: research citation card or abstract visual
- Slide 3: contrast graphic — blocked vs variable practice outcomes
- Slide 4: whiteboard drill diagram showing unpredictability
- Slide 5: neurological/simple brain diagram showing functional equivalence
- Slide 6: athlete-in-context with headphones
- Slide 7: clean statement card

**Claim-risk notes:**
- "Making you worse" is strong. Tone down in final copy to "training may not transfer" if needed.
- Ensure research citations are accurate and not overstated.

## Flow C: The Experiment, The Result, The System

**Concept name:** What If You Stopped Rehearsing Moves?

**Clickbait hero direction:**
"I stopped doing 1v1 reps for two weeks. Instead, I did this. My coach asked what changed."

**Slide count range:** 6-8 slides

**Sequential slide jobs:**
1. **Hero:** Story setup. You did the reps. You beat everyone in training. Then you tried something different. (Curiosity: what?)
2. **The experiment:** For two weeks, no blocked 1v1s. Only variable reps with random cues. Plus one mental imagery session before bed.
3. **The result:** Not overnight. But slowly, game moments felt less foreign. The read came faster. The body reacted before the mind could doubt.
4. **Why it worked — physical:** Variable practice forces the body to read before acting. The brain learns to couple perception and action under uncertainty. (Representative learning design + contextual interference)
5. **Why it worked — mental:** Mental imagery activates the same brain areas as physical movement. PETTLEP: if you imagine the game picture vividly enough, the brain treats it as real practice.
6. **The system — for you:** Step 1: replace one blocked drill with variable reps. Step 2: add one 5-minute mental imagery session. Step 3: review the read, not just the result.
7. **Product bridge (optional):** w(inner) generates personalized visualization audio for your sport, position, and scenario — so your mental reps match your real game.
8. **Close:** Your training self is real. You just have to train the right things.

**Curiosity engine:**
The story format creates suspense. Each slide reveals more of the experiment. The reader keeps going because they want to know what "this" is and whether it will work for them.

**Why this structure fits the selected angle:**
It softens the confrontation by using a narrative experiment. Good for readers who might feel defensive about being told their training is wrong. The story provides social proof and a clear system.

**Image implications:**
- Hero: athlete in action, story-driven
- Slide 2: training setup before/after
- Slide 3: game scenario showing improved decision
- Slide 4: whiteboard diagram of variable drill
- Slide 5: simple brain/imagery diagram
- Slide 6: step-by-step system card
- Slide 7: subtle product context
- Slide 8: bold closing statement

**Claim-risk notes:**
- "My coach asked what changed" is narrative, not a claim. Safe as story framing.
- Ensure the experiment is framed as illustrative, not a guarantee.
- Keep PETTLEP claims within "can support" language.

---

## Recommendation

**Flow A** is the safest and clearest. It follows the standard science-athlete structure: problem → mechanism → physical fix → mental fix → product bridge. It gives the reader a logical path without relying on narrative provocation.

**Flow B** is the most provocative and shareable. It risks stronger pushback but also higher engagement. Good if the account wants to be more confrontational.

**Flow C** is the most accessible. The story format reduces defensiveness and gives the reader a simple system to try. Best if the audience skews younger or more casual.
