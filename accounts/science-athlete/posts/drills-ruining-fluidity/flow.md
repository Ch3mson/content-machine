## Slide 2 — Cone Drill Fix

what to avoid:
watching the ball through every cone

when you dribble through cones, your eyes stay locked on the ball. cone drills teach your eyes to follow the ball. games force you to check space before it arrives.

the fix: add a single gate at the end of the line. force a shoulder check before your exit touch.

scientific source: Eldridge, Pulling & Robins (2013) — players who scan before receiving are better prepared for the next action.

## Slide 3 — Repeating the Same Pattern (Visualization)

what to avoid:
repeating the same wall pass fifty times

when you hit the same pattern over and over, your brain stores one rhythm. the game never repeats it. your body learns a single response and freezes when the situation changes.

the fix: vary the pace and angle every third rep. when you train alone, rehearse different receive scenarios in your head.

scientific source: Driskell, Copper & Moran (1994) — mental practice supports performance when it matches the real task.

## Slide 4 — Speed Ladder Fix

what to avoid:
quick feet in a straight line

speed ladders give you rapid foot contacts on a fixed path. defenders force you to slow, hold, then burst at an angle. the ladder removed the rhythm change the game demands.

the fix: set five cones in a scattered shape. slow to the cone. hold. open your hips. explode through a random gate.

scientific source: Schmidt (1975) — variable practice helps you adapt across changing situations.

## Slide 5 — Static Partner Passing

what to avoid:
passing to a partner who stands still

static passing removes the exact cue the game gives you: a live body, shrinking space, and a bad choice if you hesitate. you learn timing without pressure.

the fix: add a shadow defender or a random color call. if you train alone, rehearse the pressure picture before you sleep.

scientific source: Travassos et al. (2012) — training transfers better when it preserves the information the game gives you.
