# Writing Principles

## Source Summary

- Primary reference accounts: user-soccer-tgaskins3 (soccer mentality/identity), user-football-kingdubar (branded user voice/failure confession), user-soccer-laviedem30 (soccer training/avoid-lists), user-marcogetsfit-lifestyle (unusual micro-habits), user-aura-fit3-fitness (branded transformation lists)
- Strongest reusable lessons: The majority of the user-account references are list-first. They use a hook, then numbered/listed rules, mistakes, habits, contrasts, or fundamentals. Identity-framed hooks and failure hooks work best when they become lists, not arcs.
- Weak patterns rejected: Pure chronology stories, pure science protocol, transformation promise without tactical content, position-specific hooks that are too narrow, and slow lesson arcs that build like mini-essays.

## Voice And Rhythm

- Sentence length: Short to medium. One idea per sentence. Two sentences per slide max for tension slides. Up to 3-4 short lines for list slides.
- Slide pacing: Slide 1 hooks. Slides 2-4/6 deliver the list. Each interior slide should feel like item 1, 2, 3, etc. Tension and identity posts still need the list spine: one contrast, rule, mistake, habit, or fundamental per slide.
- Hook behavior: Name a specific player frustration or desire in the first sentence. The hook should feel personal, not universal. "You" beats "players." "Your game" beats "the game."
- Explanation style: Explain inside the list item. Do not create a separate multi-slide argument arc. Each item should be useful on its own.
- CTA or close behavior: No explicit CTA on most posts. The save impulse comes from the content being useful, not from being asked. For product posts, the close should feel like a natural next step, not an ad.

## Authority And Trust

- How the account earns trust: By saying things that are uncomfortable but true. By admitting mistakes. By being specific — naming exact behaviors, exact numbers, exact contrasts. Generic advice erodes trust instantly.
- How claims should be framed: "This is what I've seen" or "This is what separates players at the next level" — personal observation, not universal law. Avoid claiming universal truth for soccer advice.
- What makes the voice feel fake: AI-sounding sentence structures, generic motivation language, "remember that" platitudes, over-polished transitions, borrowed authority without personal experience. Anything that sounds like it was written by someone who never played.
