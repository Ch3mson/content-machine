# Copywriting Principles

## Reader Psychology

- Core desire: To know the hidden thing that separates good players from elite ones. To feel like an insider.
- Private gap: They work hard but aren't sure they're working on the right things. They suspect there's knowledge their coaches aren't giving them.
- Fear or frustration: Wasting their potential. Getting passed over. Being average despite effort.
- Status pressure: Someone else is working while they rest. Someone else wants their spot.
- Reason to save or share: The content names a specific behavior they want to remember or send to a teammate. Saves come from tactical/useful. Shares come from "this is so true" recognition or "this called me out" humor.

## Hook Logic

- Strong hooks: List promises ("3 reasons...", "5 things..."), identity promises ("How to be a..."), hidden knowledge frames ("3 things to avoid if..."), competitive pressure ("While you X, someone else Y"), vulnerability-to-authority ("I did X wrong, here's what I learned"), branded expertise frames ("Elite ball knowledge:...").
- Weak hooks: Generic questions ("Want to be a better player?"), abstract truths ("Hard work beats talent"), motivational statements that don't promise specific value.
- Curiosity drivers: Naming a specific behavior the reader probably does, then revealing the elite alternative. The gap between "what you do" and "what the best do" creates the curiosity.
- Anti-clickbait boundary: The hook must be deliverable within the post. If the hook promises "3 things that will change your game," each of the 3 things must be genuinely useful. If the hook names a player frustration, the post must address it directly. No bait-and-switch.

## Flow Logic

- Default structure: hook + list modules. Slide 1 names the list promise. Slides 2-4/6 deliver numbered or implied items: mistakes, rules, habits, contrasts, fundamentals, or steps.
- How tension should build: The list promise creates the open loop. The viewer keeps swiping because the list is incomplete, not because an essay arc is unfolding.
- How explanation should unfold: Explain inside each item. Each slide should carry its own tiny problem/fix, rule, contrast, or habit. Do not separate the argument into slow "wrong way" → "right way" → "test" → "moral" slides.
- How fixes or takeaways should land: One takeaway per list item. Each slide should feel complete enough to screenshot and save. The final slide should be the final item landing, not a separate motivational conclusion.
- How product mentions should be earned, if relevant: w(inner) should appear as one native list item or a natural tool inside an item — not as the subject of the post. "3 ways I stop guessing after training" can include a camouflaged tracking item if claim-safe.

## Editing Heuristics

- Cut every sentence that starts with "Remember that..." or "It's important to..."
- Cut every generic motivation line. If it could appear on a locker room poster, replace it.
- Replace every adverb with a specific behavior. Not "train harder" — "add 2 extra reps when you think you're done."
- If a slide has two ideas, split it into two slides.
- If a draft has a slow essay arc, convert it into a list: 3 mistakes, 3 rules, 3 habits, 3 contrasts, or 5 fundamentals.
- Read every line aloud. If it doesn't sound like something a real player would say, rewrite it.
- After writing, check: "Would I send this to a teammate?" If not, find the fake-sounding part.
- Never use a comparison word/phrase that a real player wouldn't use in conversation.
- 3 items for standard avoid/habit posts. 4-7 rules for identity posts. 1-5 contrasts for invisible-competition posts. Keep the list spine visible either way.
