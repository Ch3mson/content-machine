# Refinement Personas

## Protocol Rules

- Each persona has one job. Do not let personas overlap.
- Each critique must separate blocking failures from non-blocking improvements.
- Each critique must produce at least one concrete rewrite action.
- Existing account docs may guide audience, voice, and format, but cannot prove sport terminology is native.
- One rejected sample creates a sample note. Repeated or explicit user feedback creates a candidate preference. Only validated durable feedback becomes a locked rule in `writing.md`, `writing/principles.md`, or `design.md`.
- Resolve persona conflicts in this order: claim safety and realism, instant value, then account/platform fit.

## Persona 1: The Bored Athlete

### Invariant Job

Owns instant value, curiosity, and time-to-value.

### Account-Specific Calibration

- Target athlete: Competitive youth/club soccer player (14-22), plays at least 3x/week, wants to get to the next level (varsity, academy, college, semi-pro). May or may not currently have a path.
- Primary desire: To know the secret that separates good players from elite ones.
- Private frustration: They practice hard but aren't sure they're practicing the right things. They suspect there's hidden knowledge their coaches aren't giving them.
- What they already know: Basic soccer skills. They've been playing for years. They know generic advice ("work harder," "be first to the ball") doesn't help.
- What they are tired of hearing: "Just work harder." "Be a team player." Generic motivation posts. Coaches who repeat cliches.
- Fastest believable payoff: One thing they can change in their next training session, next game, or tonight.

### Value Equation Check

| Lever | Account-Specific Standard |
| --- | --- |
| Dream outcome | Making the team/starting XI, getting scouted, being the player coaches trust |
| Perceived likelihood | Low — they see better players and don't know what gap they need to close |
| Time delay | Must feel usable this week, not "in 5 years" |
| Effort cost | Must feel like a smarter effort, not just more effort |

### Pass Criteria

- The first slide makes the value clear within 3 seconds.
- The hook leads with curiosity plus a concrete athlete desire.
- The copy makes the performance payoff feel near-term and usable.

### Blocking Failures

- The first slide teaches before making the athlete care.
- The value is abstract, delayed, or hard to picture.
- The hook promises education but not a performance payoff.

### Non-Blocking Improvements

- Replace general soccer language with position-specific examples when it strengthens the point.
- Add a "try this in your next session" angle if the post is tactical.

### Rewrite Actions

- Move the performance payoff into the first line.
- Replace abstract benefit language with a concrete game, practice, or trial moment.
- Lower perceived effort by naming the smallest usable action.

### Strong And Weak Examples

| Weak | Stronger |
| --- | --- |
| "To be a great defender, you need to improve your positioning." | "Most defenders chase the ball. Elite defenders stay between the ball and the goal. Here's the difference." |
| "Hard work beats talent when talent doesn't work hard." | "While you're doing your 5th rep and calling it a day, the guy taking your spot is on rep 12. Just so you know." |

## Persona 2: The Account-Native Scroller

### Invariant Job

Owns account fit, platform behavior, swipe momentum, and sentence flow.

### Account-Specific Calibration

- Inspiration accounts/posts to compare against: tgaskins3 (all 5 posts), kingdubar (both posts), laviedem30 (posts 2-4), marcogetsfit (posts 2-4)
- Hook shapes to preserve: Invisible-competition contrast ("While you X, someone else Y"), identity transformation ("How to be a [charged identity]"), avoid-list ("3 things to avoid if X"), unusual micro-habits ("3 unusual [time] habits that Y"), one-percent contrast ("Why most X never Y. The 1% do Z."). Most of these must resolve as lists.
- Sentence rhythm: Short declarative opener. Then numbered/listed modules. Direct address ("you") throughout. Ends with the final item landing, not a separate moral.
- Line length: 1-2 lines per slide. Maximum readable on a phone in 3 seconds.
- Pacing between short and explanatory sentences: Tension slides (invisible-competition, identity) need all short. List slides can have one slightly longer explanatory sentence per slide.
- Problem-to-mechanism-to-fix movement: Tension → list items. Each item may contain its own tiny problem/fix. Do not spread the argument across a slow essay arc.
- Density per slide: Low. One idea, one contrast, one rule, or one habit per slide. Never two concepts fighting for attention.

### Reference-Flow Comparison

Compare the draft against the account's inspiration posts for:

- hook shape
- sentence rhythm
- line length
- pacing between short and explanatory sentences
- problem-to-mechanism-to-fix movement
- density per slide
- smoothness and swipe momentum

### Pass Criteria

- The copy feels like a valid descendant of tgaskins3 and kingdubar (the two strongest branded-user voices).
- The copy uses the reference-native list spine unless the user requested a story/arc.
- Each slide creates a reason to continue.
- The sentence flow feels smooth when read aloud.
- The copy fits the account's expected visual density.

### Blocking Failures

- The post could fit any generic sports, fitness, or motivation page.
- The post reads like a mini-essay arc instead of hook + list modules.
- The copy feels clunky, over-explained, or detached from the reference flow.
- The slide density fights the account design system.

### Non-Blocking Improvements

- Tone can be slightly more experienced/wise than tgaskins3 (who is aggressive) and slightly more constructive than kingdubar (who is cautionary). The middle ground is the sweet spot.

### Rewrite Actions

- Match the hook shape and sentence pacing of the closest approved reference.
- Convert slow arcs into hook + 3-5 listed items.
- Cut or split sentences that break swipe momentum.
- Reorder slides so problem, mechanism, and fix move in the account-native order.

### Strong And Weak Examples

| Weak | Stronger |
| --- | --- |
| "Many players don't realize the importance of recovery. Here are 3 things you should know." | "3 things to avoid if you wanna go pro. You're probably doing #2 right now." |
| "Having confidence is very important for soccer players at every level." | "How to develop an EGO. Not the annoying kind. The kind that makes coaches trust you with the ball in the 85th minute." |

## Persona 3: The Athlete Skeptic

### Invariant Job

Owns realism, terminology, claim safety, and w(inner) camouflage.

### Account-Specific Calibration

- Sports or athlete contexts covered: Soccer/club soccer (youth through college). General athletic training, mindset, competition.
- Audience level: Competitive youth/club soccer players (14-22). Some will have high-level experience. Assume at least one reader per post has played at the level you're describing.
- Terms likely to be too niche: Position-specific coaching terminology, high-level tactical jargon, overly technical fitness/sports science terms.
- Terms likely to be fake or AI-generated: Over-polished compound phrases ("differential pressure training"), abstract performance language without concrete examples.
- Claim types that require sourcing: Training frequency claims ("most pro players do X"), physiological claims, injury/recovery claims, guaranteed performance improvements.
- Product-mention risk: w(inner) integration must be native and camouflaged. Product should feel like a tool the account uses, not an endorsement.

### Flagged-Term Protocol

Only research flagged terms, not every phrase. Flag a term when it is:

- sport-specific
- technical
- abstract
- unusually polished
- likely AI-generated
- unfamiliar to a teammate or coach
- central to the slide's credibility

### Verification Ladder

1. **Coach/teammate test**
   - Would a real teammate or coach plausibly say this out loud?
   - If no, rewrite into observable performance language.

2. **External research check**
   - Search the exact phrase with the sport context.
   - Prefer coaching resources, athlete interviews, official sport education, credible training material, reputable sport-science sources, and real athlete communities.
   - Do not rely on SEO blogs or generic AI-like articles as proof.

3. **Confidence tier**
   - `Native`: clear evidence of real athlete, coach, or sport-community usage.
   - `Acceptable`: plain and understandable even if not a specialized term.
   - `Too niche`: real term but wrong for this broad account audience.
   - `AI jargon`: sounds technical but lacks real sport usage.
   - `Unverified`: cannot confirm, so rewrite simpler.

Default action: if a term is not clearly `Native` or `Acceptable`, rewrite it into plain, observable athlete language.

### Pass Criteria

- Soccer language sounds like something a teammate or coach would recognize.
- Technical claims are sourced, softened, or removed.
- w(inner) mentions feel like a natural training tool, source, or protocol extension, not an ad.

### Blocking Failures

- AI-generated sport jargon.
- Unsupported causal claims.
- Guaranteed performance, therapy, medical, scholarship, contract, or victory claims.
- Product mentions that sound like sales copy.

### Non-Blocking Improvements

- Replace academy-level jargon ("trigger pressing," "third-man runs") with player-level language ("know when to press," "make the run your teammate expects").
- If a claim about pro behavior can't be verified, frame it as observation ("I've noticed...") not fact.

### Rewrite Actions

- Replace suspect terms with observable athlete language.
- Add a source or soften the claim.
- Remove or camouflage product language until it passes `product/claim-bank.md`.

### Strong And Weak Examples

| Weak | Stronger |
| --- | --- |
| "Optimize your proprioceptive awareness for enhanced spatial orientation on the pitch." | "Know where everyone is before you get the ball. That split-second look is what separates good passes from turnovers." |
| "Professional soccer players universally follow this nutritional periodization protocol." | "Most pros eat differently on game day than training day. Not because it's fancy. Because their body needs different fuel." |
