# Writing Principles: Athlete Stories

Use this file for stable account-specific principles extracted from competitor, inspiration, and approved account examples. Status: candidate until Workflow A3 approves a non-hero sample.

## Source Summary

- Primary reference accounts: `@legendperform` and `@asdicipline` screenshot folders.
- Internal samples: LeBron and Michael Jordan flows are useful candidate examples but are not locked account proof until quality gate approval.
- Strongest reusable lessons: compact athlete facts, hidden-routine details, mental mechanism reveal, soft product bridge, quote closer.
- Weak patterns rejected: generic motivation, copied pressure CTAs, long explainer slides, unsupported extreme claims, sales copy.

## Core Principles

| Principle | What It Means | Evidence | How To Use It |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Lead with proof, not setup | The first slide should give the viewer a fact, pressure moment, or confrontation immediately. | @legendperform opens with training numbers; @asdicipline opens with direct challenge or athlete fact. | Cut "before the fame" and open on the strongest concrete detail. |
| One slide, one proof beat | Each slide should carry one main reason to believe the story. | Both reference accounts usually make each slide a single routine, claim, or status proof. | If a slide has origin, routine, and outcome, split or cut. |
| Awe comes from specifics | Numbers, places, routines, coaches, opponents, and records create more trust than adjectives. | Phelps calories/training hours, Inoue knockout rate, Jordan Rules, Akron/couch details. | Replace "worked hard" with the actual repeated behavior. |
| Hidden routine beats public glory | The account should show what happened before the arena, not only the medal or title. | Legendperform repeatedly uses unseen training details before achievement slides. | Put training, study, visualization, pressure, or recovery before legacy proof. |
| Mental edge must be earned | Visualization or belief should appear after the reader understands the physical pressure. | LeBron/MJ flows build to visualization; @asdicipline uses visualization after McGregor hardship. | Do not mention w(inner) or visualization before the athlete story earns it. |
| Identity comes before achievement | The strongest arc shows the athlete becoming the winner internally before the public proof arrives. | Ronaldo iteration feedback: the missing piece was not more facts, but the identity of a winner being forged before Ballon d'Or reality. | Let the athlete's imagination, self-belief, or rehearsed identity become the hinge of the story. Source exact internal claims when stated as fact. |
| Product follows impact | w(inner) should come after the reader sees what mental rehearsal or identity did for the athlete. | Ronaldo iteration feedback: the product slide felt more natural after showing dreams becoming reality. | Add an impact/result slide before the product bridge when the mental turn needs proof. |
| Product bridge mirrors the athlete method | w(inner) should feel like the narrator adopting the same mental rehearsal behavior. | Existing LeBron/MJ flows place product after mental turn. | Use first person briefly, with approved product wording only. |
| Quote closes the loop | The final quote should restate the post's mental idea in the athlete's own words. | @legendperform closes each post with a quote. | Do not add commentary after the quote. |
| Pressure hooks are optional, not default | Second-person challenge can work, but only when the athlete story has rivalry, humiliation, doubt, or severe conditions. | MJ flow and @asdicipline challenge slides. | Use sparingly; avoid "what's your excuse" unless the user explicitly wants a harder motivational lane. |

## Voice And Rhythm

- Sentence length: mostly short to medium. Use short sentence fragments only when they name concrete facts.
- Slide pacing: follow the current `design.md` and approved flow for slide count. At the writing level, create a slippery slope: each slide should resolve one tension and open the next.
- Hook behavior: fact-led by default; second-person challenge only for pressure/rivalry arcs.
- Explanation style: show the observable behavior first, then the meaning. Do not explain the lesson before proving it. Avoid flat fact lists by making each fact change the stakes.
- CTA or close behavior: quote closer preferred. Direct CTA-style pressure closers are not baseline.

## Authority And Trust

- The account earns trust through named athletes, concrete routines, verified statistics, public records, and primary-source quotes.
- Training routine and visualization claims need source checks before final post use.
- Product claims must use `product/claim-bank.md` and `product/app-brief.md` wording.
- The voice feels fake when it uses generic champion language, invented internal states, clinical claims, or guaranteed outcomes.

## Open Questions

- Should baseline copy lean more toward @legendperform compact facts, @asdicipline pressure challenge, or a hybrid?
- Should quote closers stay mandatory for all posts or only for story arcs with strong sourced quotes?
- Should product mention stay in a fixed late-story placement for every account post or only appear when visualization is central to the athlete story?
- How much inferred inner imagination can the account use before it needs a primary source quote or softer framing?
