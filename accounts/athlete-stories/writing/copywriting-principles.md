# Copywriting Principles: Athlete Stories

Use this file for general persuasion logic behind the account style. This file is lower authority than account-specific voice rules.

## Reader Psychology

- Core desire: feel closer to the hidden mental and training habits that separate legendary athletes from everyone else.
- Private gap: the reader believes they have potential but lacks a repeatable mental edge under pressure.
- Fear or frustration: they are tired of being told to "grind" without seeing what elite preparation actually looks like.
- Status pressure: young athletes want to compare themselves against champions without being lectured.
- Reason to save or share: a specific fact, quote, or routine reframes a known athlete and feels useful before practice or competition.

## Hook Logic

- Strong hooks:
  - A specific routine number.
  - A little-known athlete condition.
  - A rivalry, failure, or pressure fact.
  - A direct challenge when the story can support it.
- Weak hooks:
  - "Did you know" without an immediate payoff.
  - "Before the fame" setup.
  - Generic motivational questions.
  - Product-first openings.
- Curiosity drivers:
  - Hidden training conditions.
  - Mental rehearsal before public success.
  - The gap between what the athlete looked like and what they became.
  - A quote that lands harder after the story.
- Anti-clickbait boundary: the hook must be true, sourceable, and paid off by the next slide.

## Flow Logic

- Tension should build from concrete pressure into a mental mechanism.
- Explanation should unfold through scenes and behaviors, not lessons.
- Fixes or takeaways should land as implication, not instruction.
- Product mentions are earned only after the story shows mental rehearsal, visualization, confidence, focus, or pressure preparation.

## Editing Heuristics

- Cut the first sentence if it announces the topic instead of proving it.
- Replace adjectives with facts.
- Replace broad labels like "discipline" with what the athlete did every day.
- If a slide teaches a lesson and tells a story, choose the story.
- If a product line could appear in an ad, rewrite it as a native first-person training behavior.
- If the reader would not save the slide by itself, it needs a sharper fact or cleaner quote.
