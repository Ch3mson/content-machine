# Refinement Personas: Athlete Stories

Use this file to customize the three-persona refinement protocol for this account. Keep exactly three personas.

## Protocol Rules

- Each persona has one job. Do not let personas overlap.
- Each critique must separate blocking failures from non-blocking improvements.
- Each critique must produce at least one concrete rewrite action.
- Existing account docs may guide audience, voice, and format, but cannot prove sport terminology is native.
- One rejected sample creates a sample note. Repeated or explicit user feedback creates a candidate preference. Only validated durable feedback becomes a locked rule in `writing.md`, `writing/principles.md`, or `design.md`.
- Resolve persona conflicts in this order: claim safety and realism, instant value, then account/platform fit.

## Persona 1: The Bored Athlete

### Invariant Job

Owns instant value, curiosity, and time-to-value.

### Account-Specific Calibration

- Target athlete: aspiring athletes, young competitors, and sports fans who admire elite routines.
- Primary desire: find the hidden mental or preparation edge behind legendary performance.
- Private frustration: they hear generic grind advice but do not know what elite preparation actually looks like.
- What they already know: famous athletes train hard and handle pressure.
- What they are tired of hearing: empty hustle language, wellness language, and vague confidence advice.
- Fastest believable payoff: one concrete routine, pressure detail, or mental rehearsal habit they can picture using before practice or competition.

### Value Equation Check

| Lever | Account-Specific Standard |
| --- | --- |
| Dream outcome | Feel mentally closer to how legends prepare. |
| Perceived likelihood | Concrete athlete facts and sourced quotes make the edge believable. |
| Time delay | The first slide must pay off immediately. |
| Effort cost | The copy should show a mental habit, not demand a total life overhaul. |

### Pass Criteria

- The first slide makes the value clear within 3 seconds.
- The hook leads with a concrete athlete fact, pressure moment, or challenge.
- The copy makes the mental edge feel usable without becoming instructional fluff.

### Blocking Failures

- The first slide teaches before making the athlete care.
- The value is abstract, delayed, or hard to picture.
- The hook sounds like generic motivation instead of athlete-specific proof.
- The post reads like a list of facts with no suspense or emotional escalation.

### Non-Blocking Improvements

- Add a sharper number if the hook is true but low-energy.
- Move a stronger training detail earlier.
- Cut one soft explanatory sentence.

### Rewrite Actions

- Move the best proof point into the first line.
- Replace abstract benefit language with a concrete game, practice, trial, fight, race, or possession.
- Lower perceived effort by naming the mental rep or routine instead of preaching discipline.
- Make each slide create a question the next slide has to answer.

### Strong And Weak Examples

| Weak | Stronger |
| --- | --- |
| He was disciplined before he became great. | He trained six hours a day before anyone knew his name. |
| Mentality made him different. | He rehearsed the fight before he stepped into the cage. |

## Persona 2: The Account-Native Scroller

### Invariant Job

Owns account fit, platform behavior, swipe momentum, and sentence flow.

### Account-Specific Calibration

- Inspiration accounts/posts to compare against: @legendperform Posts 1-5, @asdicipline Posts 1-5, LeBron flow, Michael Jordan flow.
- Hook shapes to preserve: compact fact hook by default; second-person pressure hook only when the story demands confrontation.
- Sentence rhythm: short to medium, concrete, mostly declarative. Longer sentence allowed only for the mental-turn slide.
- Line length: readable as centered 9:16 slide text. Avoid dense explainer paragraphs.
- Pacing between short and explanatory sentences: short proof first, explanation second.
- Problem-to-mechanism-to-fix movement: pressure or hardship, identity threat, private obsession, mental rehearsal, visible impact, product bridge when earned, quote.
- Slippery-slope standard: every slide should either raise suspense, reveal the hidden mechanism, or pay off the previous slide.
- Density per slide: one proof beat. Two sentences max unless a quality-gate sample proves otherwise.
- Slide-count flexibility: do not force a 7-slide structure when compression weakens suspense, emotional payoff, or product bridge. Add slides if each added slide creates a cleaner beat.
- Emotional payoff standard: the identity reveal should land before the product appears. If one slide is carrying both emotional payoff and w(inner) mention, split it.
- Spoken-line standard: shouted, chanted, or quoted words need quotation marks so the viewer understands they are spoken, not narrator exposition.
- Visual reading constraints from `design.md`: centered white Arial Bold text with black outline on full-bleed 1080x1920 photo.

### Reference-Flow Comparison

Compare the draft against the account's inspiration posts for:

- hook shape
- sentence rhythm
- line length
- pacing between short and explanatory sentences
- problem-to-mechanism-to-fix movement
- density per slide
- smoothness and swipe momentum

### Pass Criteria

- The copy feels like a valid descendant of the chosen references without copying them.
- Each slide creates a reason to continue.
- The sentence flow feels smooth when read aloud.
- The copy fits the account's expected visual density.

### Blocking Failures

- The post could fit any generic sports, fitness, science, or motivation page.
- The copy feels clunky, over-explained, or detached from the reference flow.
- The slide density fights the account design system.
- The product slide breaks the native visual and narrative rhythm.
- The product appears before the athlete story has shown the impact of the mental mechanism.
- The flow is compressed to fit a default slide count and loses suspense, scene detail, or emotional payoff.
- The product bridge appears on the same slide as the main emotional identity reveal when separation would make the story stronger.

### Non-Blocking Improvements

- Tighten one long sentence.
- Swap a generic adjective for a specific fact.
- Move quote attribution onto the same slide if it improves closure.

### Rewrite Actions

- Match the hook shape and sentence pacing of the closest approved reference.
- Cut or split sentences that break swipe momentum.
- Reorder slides so pressure, hidden routine, mental mechanism, and outcome move in the account-native order.
- Insert an impact slide before w(inner) if the product bridge feels abrupt.
- Split overloaded slides when one beat needs atmosphere, misdirection, payoff, or a cleaner transition into product.

### Strong And Weak Examples

| Weak | Stronger |
| --- | --- |
| His discipline was unbelievable and inspiring. | No music. No tech. Just breath, feet, and pain. |
| This is why visualization matters for athletes. | He ran the possession in his mind until the game felt familiar. |

## Persona 3: The Athlete Skeptic

### Invariant Job

Owns realism, terminology, claim safety, and w(inner) camouflage.

### Account-Specific Calibration

- Sports or athlete contexts covered: basketball, soccer, combat sports, swimming, endurance, football, tennis, golf, team sports, and future athlete profiles.
- Audience level: broad athlete audience, from ambitious youth players to semi-pro or elite competitors.
- Terms likely to be too niche: sport-specific mechanics, specialized training protocols, tactical terms, and physiology claims.
- Terms likely to be fake or AI-generated: polished phrases like "identity programming," "unshakeable mindset," "neural edge," or clinical-sounding performance claims.
- Phrase-risk nuance: common first-person expressions like "rewired my brain" can be acceptable when they sound natural and do not make clinical promises. The bigger risk is pairing technical language with empty abstractions such as "for greatness."
- Claim types that require sourcing: training routines, visualization habits, diet, sleep, quantified achievements, injury/recovery, and athlete quotes.
- Inner-life claim risk: exact childhood imagination, private self-talk, and scenes the athlete supposedly pictured need sourcing or softer framing.
- Product-mention risk: w(inner) must sound like a mental reps tool, not a sponsor or guarantee.

### Flagged-Term Protocol

Only research flagged terms, not every phrase. Flag a term when it is:

- sport-specific
- technical
- abstract
- unusually polished
- likely AI-generated
- unfamiliar to a teammate or coach
- central to the slide's credibility

### Verification Ladder

1. **Coach/teammate test**
   - Would a real teammate or coach plausibly say this out loud?
   - If no, rewrite into observable performance language.
2. **External research check**
   - Search the exact phrase with the sport context.
   - Prefer coaching resources, athlete interviews, official sport education, credible training material, reputable sport-science sources, and real athlete communities.
   - Do not rely on SEO blogs or generic AI-like articles as proof.
3. **Confidence tier**
   - `Native`: clear evidence of real athlete, coach, or sport-community usage.
   - `Acceptable`: plain and understandable even if not a specialized term.
   - `Too niche`: real term but wrong for this broad account audience.
   - `AI jargon`: sounds technical but lacks real sport usage.
   - `Unverified`: cannot confirm, so rewrite simpler.

Default action: if a term is not clearly `Native` or `Acceptable`, rewrite it into plain, observable athlete language.

### Pass Criteria

- Sport language sounds like something a teammate or coach would recognize.
- Technical claims are sourced, softened, or removed.
- w(inner) mentions feel like a natural extension of the athlete's mental preparation.
- Product language uses approved claims only.

### Blocking Failures

- AI-generated sport jargon.
- Unsupported causal claims.
- Unsourced claims about what the athlete imagined, believed, or privately rehearsed stated as fact.
- Guaranteed performance, therapy, medical, scholarship, contract, or victory claims.
- Product mentions that sound like sales copy.

### Non-Blocking Improvements

- Replace a polished phrase with a simpler locker-room phrase.
- Mark a strong fact for source verification.
- Swap a product noun for a training behavior.

### Rewrite Actions

- Replace suspect terms with observable athlete language.
- Add a source or soften the claim.
- Remove or camouflage product language until it passes `product/claim-bank.md`.
- Reframe unsourced inner-life claims as narrative interpretation, or anchor them to sourced quotes/interviews.

### Strong And Weak Examples

| Weak | Stronger |
| --- | --- |
| w(inner) unlocks clutch performance. | I use w(inner) for mental reps before the moment gets loud. |
| He rewired his nervous system for greatness. | He rehearsed the moment until it felt familiar. |
| He was chanting the future. | He screamed like he was predicting the future. |
