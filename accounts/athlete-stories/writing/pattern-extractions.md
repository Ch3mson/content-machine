## Screenshot Transcript Notes

### @legendperform

Common slide subjects across five posts:

- Extreme routine numbers: calories, daily training hours, wake time, repeated sessions, win streaks.
- Sensory restriction and hidden-work details: no mirrors, no comfort, no music, no tech, same meals, breath and pain.
- Mechanism details: film study, altitude chamber, timing, balance, footwork, explosive throws.
- Status proof: Olympic golds, world records, undefeated status, world championships, decorated legacy.
- Quote closer: one athlete quote, usually broad enough to convert fact admiration into identity reflection.

Representative transcript excerpts:

- Phelps: ate up to 10,000 calories, trained six hours a day, altitude chamber, 23 Olympic gold medals, quote about limits.
- Dressel: wakes at 4:30 AM, no phone until 10 AM, no mirrors/no comfort, film study, ice baths, quote about winning races in the dark.
- Cheptegei: trains three times a day, dirt road in Uganda, simple meals, no music or tech, world record holder, mental strength quote.
- Inoue: knockout percentage, punching power, trains speed/timing/balance, undefeated status, speed and timing quote.
- Riner: ten years without losing, 154 wins in a row, judo plus sprinting plus gym, moves like a lightweight, suffering quote.

## Viewer-Perspective Notes

### What makes the viewer stop

- A number that feels larger than expected.
- A detail that reveals private discipline instead of public achievement.
- A direct question that tests the reader's self-image.
- A famous athlete shown through a less familiar routine.

### What private frustration or desire gets named

- The viewer wants proof that elite athletes are built through repeatable habits.
- The viewer wants to feel closer to a champion's hidden routine.
- Young competitors want pressure to feel trainable, not mystical.
- The viewer is tired of vague motivation and wants a concrete edge.

### What promise keeps the viewer swiping

- Each slide reveals another hidden proof point.
- The post implies: if you understand the routine, you understand the athlete.
- Quote closers give the post save/share value.

### Where trust comes from

- Specific athletes, numbers, routines, opponents, coaches, and outcomes.
- Observable training behaviors instead of abstract mindset claims.
- Known public records when the slide reaches legacy/status proof.

### What feels copied, generic, or weak

- "No excuses" and "what's your excuse" endings.
- Long explainer paragraphs that fight slideshow readability.
- Emoji-led rule lists.
- Unsupported big claims presented as facts.
- Motivational metaphors that could fit any athlete.

## Candidate Patterns

- Open with proof, not setup.
- Use one athlete-specific fact per slide.
- Let numbers create awe, then use mechanism details to create trust.
- Move from hidden routine to mental mechanism to visible outcome.
- Use quote closers when the quote reinforces the post's mental edge.
- Use second-person pressure only for athletes whose story justifies confrontation.

## Rejected Patterns

- Generic hustle slogans.
- Text-heavy educational rules unless the account intentionally runs a list/explainer format.
- Unverified routine claims without source checks.
- Sales-style product bridges.
- Direct competitor wording or slide-for-slide copying.
