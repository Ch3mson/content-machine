## Extracted Pattern: Biological ROI Ranking

### Why The Viewer Stops

The phrase `biological ROI` reframes ordinary habits as investments in the
athlete's nervous system. A ranked list adds curiosity because the reader wants
to know which habit has the highest return and whether their current routine is
wasting effort.

### Private Gap It Names

The athlete trains hard but does not know which habits actually change the
system that fails under pressure. For the validated soccer post, the private gap
was: physical warmup is routine, but mental warmup feels like superstition or
guesswork.

### Emotional Lever

Confidence from preparation, not motivation. The strongest version targets the
gap between practice confidence and game-day confidence: the athlete knows they
have the skill but cannot always access it when the pressure starts.

### Mechanism In Play

Each ranked item needs a biological or cognitive mechanism: classical
conditioning, cognitive interference, HPA-axis regulation, motor imagery,
repeated sprint exposure, attention control, procedural memory, or another
source-backed pathway. The mechanism must convert into a concrete sport result.

### Flow Structure

1. Hero: `5 [sport-specific habits] ranked by biological ROI:` plus a mechanism
   promise in parentheses.
2. Body slides: ranked from `#5` to `#1`.
3. Each body slide: habit title, `the biological roi (why it works):`, mechanism
   explanation, centered photo/visual frame, `the result:`, source line.
4. Optional final stack slide only when the ranked items become a usable routine
   or product/tool handoff.

### Fix Direction

Turn the ranked list into a repeatable protocol the athlete can test in the next
practice, game, trial, or tournament. When product fit is natural, w(inner) can
appear as a tool for personalized visualization audio by sport, position, and
specific moment.

### Design Implications

The pattern fits the locked athlete-max body slide system:

- white background
- centered label/title/mechanism
- fixed `800x450` image frame
- lower `the result:` block
- source footer
- B&W or neutral imagery

### Risks

- A ranking without evidence feels arbitrary.
- `biological ROI` can become empty jargon if the slide does not explain the
  mechanism.
- Product integration can become ad-like if w(inner) is presented as the point of
  the post instead of one tool inside the routine.
- Broad neuroscience claims need sources or softer wording.
