## What This Slide Tests

- Whether the TAP-R mechanism formula produces copy that is both educational and native.
- Whether "conditioning problem" reads more naturally than "leg problem" to a soccer player.
- Whether naming the motor cortex with a parenthetical anchor satisfies the user's request for terminology + educational value.
- Whether embedding the payoff inside the fix block (instead of separate sections) improves flow.
- Whether an existing individual drill (wall passes) with one dramatic constraint feels more actionable than a generic invented drill.
- Whether the 9-word minimum per rendered line holds at 30px body text and 48px title.

## Writing Principles Being Tested

- Problem-first hook in plain language; value clear in 2 seconds.
- Desire-before-information: mechanism converted to a tangible in-match payoff.
- One reader across the slide.
- Mechanism before protocol (motor cortex suppression → wall-pass constraint).
- Specificity (30-40 cm, minute 70, minute 80, 10 minutes, every third touch, 23%).
- No motivational language; no bro-science; no Andrew Tate framing.
- Performance translation explicit.
- TAP-R: Term → Anchor → Process → Result, with Why Resolution in the same breath.
