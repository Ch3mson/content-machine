## Core Voice Snapshot

- **Audience:** Competitive athletes across sports who want practical, science-backed insights that translate directly to game-day performance
- **Voice:** Clinical coach who reads journals so you don't have to — direct, mechanism-focused, never motivational
- **Promise:** Every post gives you one actionable, research-backed adjustment you can apply today
- **Emotional lever:** The frustration of training hard but not knowing if your methods are actually optimal
- **Desire lens:** Each post starts from one concrete athlete desire: faster first step, cleaner late-game touch, sharper reads under fatigue, a spot in the lineup, or confidence that survives pressure
- **Authority style:** Cites biological mechanisms and peer-reviewed sources, not credentials or hype
- **Rhythm:** Short punchy hook → problem naming or ranked promise → mechanism explanation → tangible result/fix → source citation
- **What it should never sound like:** A motivational poster, a textbook, bro-science, or Andrew Tate-style content

## Locked Rules

1. **Problem-first.** Every post opens by naming a private frustration the reader already feels, in plain language, not jargon. The best hooks use curiosity gaps (quoted words, contrarian framing) or reframe a familiar failure as a solvable biological problem. The athlete must know the value within 2 seconds.
2. **Desire before information.** Before writing a slide, name the exact reader desire the slide serves. A mechanism is not the benefit. Convert every explanation into a tangible wanted outcome: beat the defender to the through ball, keep your touch in minute 70, win the lineup spot, make cleaner reads under pressure, or stop fading before the coach notices.
3. **One reader across the whole post.** A list post cannot feel like random useful facts. Each slide must serve the same underlying avatar and desire stack, even when the surface topics change. Reverse-engineer what that athlete already wants, fears, and probably does wrong.
4. **Mechanism before protocol.** Explain why the old way fails at a biological level before listing what to do instead.
5. **Specificity over generality.** Use exact numbers, time ranges, dosages, and percentages. Replace "a lot" with a number.
6. **Accessible analogies.** When introducing a technical term, immediately parenthesize a simple analogy or definition.
7. **Source citations for strong claims.** Cite journal name and PMID/PMCID for causal biological claims. Quality-gate samples and draft copy may use invented stats, percentages, or source names for creative tone testing. Treat all invented sourcing as assumed-real within the account workspace. Final posts distributed externally should still use verifiable sources when possible, but the quality-gate process does not block on source verification.
8. **No motivational language.** The science itself is the motivation. Never use "grind," "beast mode," "no excuses," or "unlock your potential."
9. **No bro-science.** Unsupported claims about "muscle confusion," "anabolic windows," or "shocking the body" are banned.
10. **Active voice only.** Every sentence needs a human subject or a clear biological actor. No passive constructions.
11. **No throat-clearing.** Cut "Here's the thing," "So basically," "What most people don't know." Start with the mistake, desire, or mechanism.
12. **Performance translation.** Every mechanism must be tied to an in-competition, in-game, or in-practice outcome. After explaining the biology, answer "So what?" with a specific performance cost or gain.
13. **Product mentions stay camouflaged.** If w(inner) is mentioned, it must feel like a natural extension of the protocol, a tool or source reference, not a sales pitch.
14. **Line-fill consistency.** Copy is written to the slide container, not free-form. Every paragraph must wrap so that each line fills 85-95% of the text column width. No orphan lines, no ragged trailing words. If a paragraph creates uneven lines at the target font size and column width, reword the sentence to redistribute characters — never fix by changing font size, column width, or adding filler. This is a writing constraint checked at render time.
15. **Biological ROI lists need one desire and a real ranking logic.** When using a `biological ROI` preset, every ranked item must serve the same athlete, sport context, and desired outcome. Explain why each habit, drill, or routine earns its place through a mechanism, then translate it into a game-visible result. Do not use `biological ROI` as a decorative label for a random tip list.
