## Approved Samples

### Sample 1: Problem-First Mechanism Post (chasingpeaks0 style)

- **Source:** Adapted from chasingpeaks0 Post 1, rethemed for competitive athletic performance
- **Why it works:** The hook uses quotes to create an immediate curiosity gap — every athlete has felt "slower" in games but could not explain why. The post reframes this familiar confidence-killer as a solvable sleep problem, not a mental weakness. The mechanism ties directly to reads, reactions, and clutch performance. No jargon in the hook; plain language only.
- **Principles shown:** Curiosity-gap hook, performance translation, mechanism-before-protocol, accessible analogies, specific numbers
- **Design or format notes:** Dark moody background for hook/problem slides; clean background for fix slides. Bold key terms. Use emoji arrows or numbered circles for scannability.

```text
Slide 1 (Hook):
Why Your Body Moves "Slower"
in Games Than in Practice

Slide 2 (Problem):
You know the play. You drilled it 200 times.
In practice you react without thinking.

Then game day comes and your body does not do
what you trained it to do. You hesitate on reads.
Your first step is late. You second-guess yourself
and lose confidence.

Everyone tells you it is "nerves." It is not.
➡️ Scrolling until 1 am the night before
➡️ Sleeping in a room above 70°F
➡️ Eating within 2 hours of sleep
➡️ Using alcohol to "wind down"

These habits cut the exact sleep phase your brain
uses to hard-wire motor patterns and clear the
chemical that makes you feel sluggish. >>>

Slide 3 (Mechanism):
Your brain does not learn the play during practice.
It learns it while you sleep.

During slow-wave sleep, your brain replays motor
patterns at 20x normal speed. This is when a new
defensive read, technique, or reaction gets
hard-wired into your procedural memory — the same
system that lets you ride a bike without thinking.

It also clears adenosine from your prefrontal
cortex, the area that handles split-second decisions
when the game is close and the pressure is high.

When you cut sleep by even 90 minutes, you do not
just feel tired. You reduce next-day reaction time
by 10–15%. You increase error rate on complex
decisions by 20–30%. That is the difference between
making the read and getting beat.

Slide 4 (The Fix — numbered):
How to sleep so your body does what you trained:

1. Anchor sleep to solar time
Sleep 11 pm–7 am aligns slow-wave sleep with your
lowest core body temperature. This is when the
brain replays motor patterns at full speed.
Sleeping 3 am–11 am misses that window. Your brain
cannot "bank" sleep. If you miss the window, the
play does not get hard-wired.

2. 60–90 min of low light before bed
Blue light suppresses melatonin. Low light tells
your brain night has arrived, deepening sleep onset
and extending the first slow-wave cycle — the one
that consolidates the motor patterns you practiced
that day.

3. Cool room (65–68°F)
Research shows this increases slow-wave sleep.
More slow-wave sleep = faster motor pattern
consolidation and less adenosine buildup. You wake
up with sharper decision speed and a first step
that actually shows up in games.

4. Front-load calories earlier
A large meal close to bed raises core temperature
and activates digestion when your brain should be
in memory-replay mode. Eat your last substantial
meal 3+ hours before sleep.

Slide 5 (What to stop doing):
➡️ "Sleeping in" to recover from late nights
Your circadian window is fixed. Sleeping until
noon does not restore the slow-wave cycles you
missed between 11 pm and 3 am. Your brain is still
carrying yesterday's adenosine into the game.

➡️ Using alcohol to fall asleep
It sedates you but fragments REM and suppresses
growth hormone. You wake up feeling rested but
your motor patterns did not consolidate. In warmups
you look fine. In the fourth quarter, when decision
load peaks, your body does not do what you trained
it to do.

Slide 6 (Source):
source: nature neuroscience / nih -
"sleep and motor skill consolidation" (pmid: 12536210)

additional source: sleep medicine reviews
"the effects of partial sleep deprivation on
reaction time and executive function" (pmid: 32438209)
```

### Sample 2: "What to Avoid" → "The Fix" Post (verified_byhumans style)

- **Source:** Adapted from verified_byhumans Post 1, rethemed for competitive athletic performance
- **Why it works:** The hook names a physical sensation every competitor has felt: heavy legs in the final period. The benefit is not "learn conditioning science." The benefit is staying explosive when the opponent fades. Each body slide should make that payoff concrete in the athlete's world: cleaner cuts, sharper reads, repeat sprints, and fewer late-game mistakes.
- **Principles shown:** Curiosity-gap hook, actionable binary, performance translation, source citations, clean scannability
- **Design or format notes:** White background, black text, red accent on key phrases or warnings. B&W photography. Lowercase headings optional — test in quality gate.

```text
Slide 1 (Hook):
Why Your Legs Feel Heavy
in the 4th Quarter

Slide 2 (What to avoid):
what to avoid:
running nothing but hard sprints in practice

when every session is a max-effort sprint, your
body gets better at burning fuel fast but worse
at making new fuel. your anaerobic system grows
while your aerobic system shrinks. you can blast
for 10 seconds, but between plays your body cannot
clear lactate or refill energy stores.

the performance cost: in the fourth quarter,
your opponents are still bouncing between plays.
your legs feel heavy. your cuts get slower. your
reads get late. you are not out of shape. you are
out of fuel systems.

Slide 3 (The Fix):
the fix: the 80/20 rule

80% of your weekly running at low intensity
(below your first ventilatory threshold — you
should be able to hold a conversation).

20% at high intensity (above your second
ventilatory threshold — short, maximal sprints).

this ratio builds the aerobic base that clears
lactate between plays and refills energy stores
so you can repeat sprints in the final minutes
with the same speed you had in warmups.

scientific source: international journal of
sports physiology and performance / nih -
"polarized training has greater impact on key
endurance variables than threshold or high-
intensity training." (pmid: 30452750)

Slide 4 (What to avoid):
what to avoid:
practicing dehydrated

you lose water overnight. even 2% body-mass loss
impairs thermoregulation, reduces plasma volume,
and increases heart rate at the same workload.

the performance cost: your brain runs on glucose
and water. even mild dehydration slows
information processing speed and working memory —
the exact systems you use to read a defense or
adjust to a set play in real time. you do not
feel thirsty. you just process slower.

the fix: 500 ml water + electrolytes within
30 min of waking. this restores plasma volume
and buffers the cortisol spike that peaks
immediately after waking. your brain rehydrates
before your first drill.

scientific source: journal of the international
society of sports nutrition / nih -
"effects of dehydration on exercise performance"
(pmid: 20799932)

additional source: medicine & science in sports
& exercise / nih - "mild dehydration impairs
cognitive performance and mood" (pmid: 21738326)
```

## Candidate Samples

### Candidate 1: Biological ROI Hobby Post

- **Source:** Adapted from verified_byhumans Post 2, rethemed for athlete cross-training
- **What works:** The "biological ROI" frame turns cross-training from a chore into an investment. Each hobby is self-contained and cites a specific neurological or physiological benefit.
- **What needs revision:** The hobby choices need to be athlete-specific (e.g., replace "public speaking" with "yoga for HRV" or "cold exposure for norepinephrine"). The evolutionary-psychology lines ("subconsciously, humans read...") need to be cut or heavily sourced.

## Rejected Samples

- Any post that opens with a motivational quote or "The grind never stops."
- Any post that lists "3 superfoods" without explaining the active compound and mechanism.
- Any post that uses "alpha" or "sigma" framing.
- Any post that explains a mechanism but never turns it into an instant reader benefit.
- Any list post where the slides serve different avatars instead of one consistent desire stack.
