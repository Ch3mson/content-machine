# Sample Posts

Use this file for approved or candidate examples that show the writing system in
practice. Samples explain principles; they are not templates to copy blindly.

## Persona-Driven Rewrite Notes (2026-04-28)

After running the three refinement personas against the previous drafts:

**The Bored Athlete** flagged that Slide 3 (Mechanism) in Sample 1 front-loaded
technical explanation before cementing the payoff. The athlete was asked to learn
about mechanisms before fully understanding what they get. Each slide must give
a reason to care before it asks them to learn.

**The Account-Native Scroller** flagged that density in Sample 1 Slide 3 fights
swipe momentum. Technical terms in sequence break the clean, scannable flow.
Sample 2's use of "ventilatory threshold" broke the verified_byhumans-style
simplicity.

**The Athlete Skeptic** flagged the following terms as too niche for a broad
competitive athlete audience:
- "suprachiasmatic nucleus" → rewrite as "the part of your brain that tracks
  day and night"
- "adenosine" → rewrite as "the chemical that makes you feel sluggish"
- "prefrontal cortex" → rewrite as "the part that makes split-second decisions"
- "ventilatory threshold" → rewrite as "pace where you can hold a conversation"
- "plasma volume" → rewrite as "the fluid your heart needs to pump"
- "thermoregulation" → rewrite as "your body's ability to cool down"
- "cortisol" → rewrite as "stress hormone" or remove
- "anaerobic/aerobic system" → rewrite as "sprint system/endurance system"

**Rewrite rule applied:** If a term is not something a teammate would say in the
locker room, remove it or replace it with the performance consequence.

## Approved Samples

### Sample 1: Problem-First Mechanism Post (chasingpeaks0 style)

- **Source:** Adapted from chasingpeaks0 Post 1, rethemed for competitive athletic
  performance
- **Why it works:** The hook names a private frustration every competitive
  athlete has felt — playing worse in games than in practice. The problem slide
  reframes "nerves" as a solvable sleep problem in plain language. Every
  technical term has been replaced with observable performance language. The
  mechanism explains why the body feels slower, then immediately translates to
  reads, first steps, and fourth-quarter decisions. The fix gives four bounded
  actions with specific time ranges. No jargon appears before the athlete
  understands what they get.
- **Principles shown:** Curiosity-gap hook, performance translation,
  mechanism-before-protocol, plain-language mandate, specific numbers
- **Design or format notes:** Dark moody background for hook/problem slides;
  clean background for fix slides. Bold key terms. Use emoji arrows or numbered
  circles for scannability.

```text
Slide 1 (Hook):
Why Your Body Moves "Slower"
in Games Than in Practice

Slide 2 (Problem):
You know the play. You drilled it 200 times.
In practice you react without thinking.

Then game day comes and your body does not do
what you trained it to do. You hesitate on reads.
Your first step is late. You second-guess yourself
and lose confidence.

Everyone tells you it is "nerves." It is not.

These habits cut the exact sleep your brain uses
to hard-wire skills:

➡️ Scrolling until 1 am the night before
➡️ Sleeping in a room above 70°F
➡️ Eating within 2 hours of sleep
➡️ Using alcohol to "wind down"

Slide 3 (Mechanism):
Your brain does not learn the play during practice.
It learns it while you sleep.

While you sleep, your brain replays the plays you
practiced that day at 20x speed. This is when a
new read or reaction gets locked in — the same
way you learn to ride a bike without thinking.

Your brain also clears the chemical that makes
you feel sluggish from the decision-making part
that handles pressure.

Cut sleep by even 90 minutes and your reaction
time drops 10–15% the next day. Your error rate
on complex decisions rises 20–30%.

That is the difference between making the read
and getting beat.

Slide 4 (The Fix):
How to sleep so your body does what you trained:

1. Sleep 11 pm–7 am
This aligns deep sleep with your lowest body
temperature. This is when your brain replays
plays at full speed. Sleep 3 am–11 am and you
miss the window. Your brain cannot "bank" sleep.

2. 60–90 min of low light before bed
Blue light blocks the hormone that tells your
brain it is night. Low light deepens sleep and
extends the first cycle — the one that hard-wires
the skills you practiced that day.

3. Cool room (65–68°F)
Research shows this increases deep sleep. More
deep sleep = faster skill hard-wiring and less
sluggishness the next day. You wake up with
sharper decision speed.

4. Front-load calories earlier
A large meal close to bed raises body temperature
and activates digestion when your brain should be
in replay mode. Eat your last big meal 3+ hours
before sleep.

Slide 5 (What to stop doing):
➡️ "Sleeping in" to recover from late nights
Your sleep window is fixed. Sleeping until noon
does not restore the deep cycles you missed
between 11 pm and 3 am. Your brain is still
carrying yesterday's sluggishness into the game.

➡️ Using alcohol to fall asleep
It knocks you out but fragments the sleep that
hard-wires skills. You wake up feeling rested but
your reads did not get locked in. You look fine
in warmups. In the fourth quarter, when decisions
matter most, your body does not do what you
trained it to do.

Slide 6 (Source):
source: nature neuroscience / nih -
"sleep and motor skill consolidation" (pmid: 12536210)

additional source: sleep medicine reviews
"the effects of partial sleep deprivation on
reaction time and executive function" (pmid: 32438209)
```

### Sample 2: "What to Avoid" → "The Fix" Post (verified_byhumans style)

- **Source:** Adapted from verified_byhumans Post 1, rethemed for competitive
  athletic performance
- **Why it works:** The hook names a physical sensation every competitor has
  felt — heavy legs in the final period. The "what to avoid" slide explains the
  mistake in plain language: sprinting too much makes you worse at making fuel.
  The 80/20 fix removes technical thresholds and uses a simple conversational
  test. The dehydration section replaces lab terms with observable consequences.
  Every slide passes the locker-room test: a teammate could read it aloud and
  every athlete would understand.
- **Principles shown:** Curiosity-gap hook, actionable binary, performance
  translation, plain-language mandate, source citations, clean scannability
- **Design or format notes:** White background, black text, red accent on key
  phrases or warnings. B&W photography. Lowercase headings optional — test in
  quality gate.

```text
Slide 1 (Hook):
Why Your Legs Feel Heavy
in the 4th Quarter

Slide 2 (What to avoid):
what to avoid:
running nothing but hard sprints in practice

when every session is a max-effort sprint, your
body gets better at burning fuel fast but worse
at making new fuel. your sprint system grows
while your endurance system shrinks.

you can blast for 10 seconds, but between plays
your body cannot clear the burning feeling or
refill energy stores.

the performance cost: in the fourth quarter,
your opponents are still bouncing between plays.
your legs feel heavy. your cuts get slower. your
reads get late. you are not out of shape. you are
out of fuel systems.

Slide 3 (The Fix):
the fix: the 80/20 rule

80% of your weekly running at a pace where you
can hold a conversation.

20% at short, all-out sprints.

this ratio builds the endurance base that clears
the burning feeling between plays and refills
energy stores so you can repeat sprints in the
final minutes with the same speed you had in
warmups.

scientific source: international journal of
sports physiology and performance / nih -
"polarized training has greater impact on key
endurance variables than threshold or high-
intensity training." (pmid: 30452750)

Slide 4 (What to avoid):
what to avoid:
practicing dehydrated

you lose water overnight. even 2% body weight loss
makes it harder to cool down, reduces the fluid
your heart needs to pump, and makes your heart
work harder for the same output.

the performance cost: your brain runs on glucose
and water. even mild dehydration slows how fast
you process what you see and how many details you
can hold at once — the exact systems you use to
read a defense or adjust to a set play in real
time. you do not feel thirsty. you just process
slower.

the fix: 500 ml water + electrolytes within
30 min of waking. this restores the fluid your
heart needs and buffers the stress hormone spike
that hits immediately after waking. your brain
rehydrates before your first drill.

scientific source: journal of the international
society of sports nutrition / nih -
"effects of dehydration on exercise performance"
(pmid: 20799932)

additional source: medicine & science in sports
& exercise / nih - "mild dehydration impairs
cognitive performance and mood" (pmid: 21738326)
```

## Candidate Samples

### Candidate 1: Biological ROI Cross-Training Post

- **Source:** Adapted from verified_byhumans Post 2, rethemed for athlete
  cross-training
- **What works:** The "biological ROI" frame turns cross-training from a chore
  into an investment. Each activity is self-contained and cites a specific
  neurological or physiological benefit.
- **What needs revision:** The activity choices need to be athlete-specific
  (e.g., yoga for HRV, cold exposure for norepinephrine, rucking for posture).
  Every slide must pass the locker-room test. No evolutionary-psychology lines
  without heavy sourcing.

## Rejected Samples

- Any post that opens with a motivational quote or "The grind never stops."
- Any post that lists "3 superfoods" without explaining the active compound and
  mechanism.
- Any post that uses "alpha" or "sigma" framing.
- Any post that uses technical terms in the first two slides before the athlete
  understands what they get.
