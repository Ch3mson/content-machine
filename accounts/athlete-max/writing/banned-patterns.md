# Banned Patterns

Use this file for account-specific weak moves, banned phrases, and replacements.

## Banned Moves

| Pattern | Why It Fails | Replacement |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Motivational fluff** | The audience is explicitly tired of "grind" and "no excuses" content. It signals low information density. | Replace with mechanism + outcome: instead of "Push through the pain," use "Your pain tolerance is trainable through gradual exposure — here is the HPA-axis mechanism." |
| **Bro-science claims** | Unsupported claims about "muscle confusion," "shocking the body," or "anabolic windows" destroy trust with a science-literate audience. | Replace with peer-reviewed mechanisms: "Progressive overload increases motor unit recruitment, which is why load progression outperforms random variation." |
| **Jargon without explanation** | Dropping terms like "mTOR pathway" or "autophagy" without defining them makes the account feel like a textbook or a poser. | Introduce the term with an analogy: "mTOR (the cellular switch that tells your muscles to build new protein)" |
| **Unsourced absolutes** | Words like "always," "never," "guaranteed," "proven" without a source feel like marketing, not science. | Soften to "research suggests," "is associated with," or add a source citation. |
| **Generic listicles without mechanism** | "5 foods that boost testosterone" without explaining the biological pathway feels like clickbait. | Structure as "5 foods that influence testosterone via the [specific mechanism] pathway" |
| **Andrew Tate-style framing** | Aggressive status competition, misogynistic undertones, or "alpha/beta" language alienates the target audience and conflicts with the clinical voice. | Replace with evolutionary or biological framing that is neutral and evidence-based: "Social isolation spikes cortisol because humans evolved as tribal mammals" |
| **Throat-clearing openers** | "Here's the thing," "So basically," "What most people don't know" waste the first line and signal AI-generated content. | Cut to the point. Start with the mistake or the mechanism. |
| **Passive voice** | "The muscle is activated" removes the actor and feels clinical in a bad way — distant and impersonal. | Use active voice: "Your brain recruits the muscle" |
| **Vague declaratives** | "The implications are significant" or "This is important" tells the reader what to think instead of showing them why. | Name the specific implication: "This means your recovery window closes after 90 minutes, not 24 hours." |
| **Coaching shorthand as copy** | Words that require the reader to already know what a coach means — "read," "touch," "look," "decision" used bare — fail the locker-room test. A scroller does not parse "hesitate before the easy read." They see a pass they should have made. | Replace every shorthand with the visible physical action: "see the opening and do not take it," "spot the space and stop attacking," "hold the ball instead of moving it." Same rule for "play smaller" / "step up" / "slow down" used bare. A teammate says "I saw the pass and didn't throw it," not "my reads were off." Write what they say. |
| **Over-promising outcomes** | "Unlock your full potential" or "Transform your body in 30 days" sets expectations the science cannot meet. | Promise specific, bounded outcomes: "Increase slow-wave sleep by 15-20% through temperature regulation" |
| **Mechanism as the benefit** | Explaining biology without translating it into a wanted outcome makes the slide feel educational but not desirable. | Tie the mechanism to a concrete moment: "you beat the center back to the near-post run in minute 70" instead of "your aerobic system improves." |
| **Orphan and ragged lines** | A text block where lines are wildly uneven in length — some full-width, others trailing off with 2-4 words — looks amateur and breaks the dense authority rhythm of the reference accounts. Center-aligned text with uneven lines creates a diamond/triangle shape instead of a clean rectangle. | Rewrite the sentence to redistribute words so every line fills 85-95% of the column width. Test against the actual font and column width before approving. Never pad with filler words — restructure the sentence instead. |

## Banned Phrases

- "Beast mode"
- "No excuses"
- "Grind"
- "Alpha"
- "Sigma"
- "Shock your muscles"
- "Confuse the body"
- "Unlock your potential"
- "The science is clear" (without citing the science)
- "Experts agree" (without naming the experts or studies)
- "Here's what/this/that" (throat-clearing)
- "At the end of the day"
- "The bottom line is"
- "Easy read" / "hard read" / any "read" used bare without a physical action
- "Play smaller" / "step up" / "slow down" / any abstract performance metaphor used without naming the visible action

## Weak Structures

- **Three-item lists for their own sake:** If there are only 2 real items, use 2. If there are 7, use 7. Do not force a 3-item structure.
- **Binary "not X, it's Y" contrasts:** These feel formulaic. State Y directly.
- **Dramatic fragmentation:** Short. Choppy. Sentences. For. Effect. This reads as try-hard.
- **Rhetorical questions as hooks:** "Did you know that...?" feels like a children's science show. State the fact directly.
- **Meta-joiners:** "The rest of this post will explain..." Let the post move without announcing itself.
- **Random useful list:** A list of tips without one consistent reader desire loses immersion. Pick the avatar and desire stack first.

## Cleanup Checklist

- [ ] Remove generic motivation.
- [ ] Remove empty throat-clearing.
- [ ] Remove unsupported claims.
- [ ] Remove copy that sounds like a template instead of the account.
- [ ] Remove any passive voice.
- [ ] Remove any adverbs (very, really, extremely, actually, basically).
- [ ] Remove any "here's what/this/that" openers.
- [ ] Ensure every strong claim has a source or is softened.
- [ ] Ensure every technical term has an analogy or definition on first use.
- [ ] Ensure every mechanism becomes a tangible reader benefit on the same slide.
