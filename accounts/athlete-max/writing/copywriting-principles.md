# Copywriting Principles

Use this file for general persuasion logic behind the account style. This file
is lower authority than account-specific voice rules.

## Reader Psychology

- **Core desire:** To gain a measurable, science-backed edge over competitors without wasting time on ineffective methods.
- **Private gap:** The fear that they are training hard but doing the wrong things — that their effort is not translating into performance because of invisible biological mistakes.
- **Fear or frustration:** Wasting training years on bro-science, hearing contradictory advice from coaches, not knowing which recovery or nutrition hacks are real.
- **Status pressure:** Competitive athletes are constantly compared. Saving and sharing science-backed posts is a low-risk way to signal intelligence and dedication.
- **Reason to save or share:** The post contains a specific protocol, mechanism, or source they want to reference later or show to a coach/teammate.

## Hook Logic

- **Strong hooks:** Name a specific mistake ("You are probably recovering wrong"), promise a biological upgrade ("The 60/60 rule for permanent hearing protection"), or reframe a common habit as dangerous ("Mouth breathing while distracted is destroying your jawline").
- **Weak hooks:** Generic listicles ("3 tips to run faster"), motivational openers ("The grind never stops"), vague curiosity gaps ("You won't believe what this study found").
- **Curiosity drivers:** The mechanism itself is the curiosity driver. "Your brain stores language in two different memory systems" is more compelling than "This one trick will help you learn faster."
- **Anti-clickbait boundary:** The hook must deliver exactly what it promises. If the hook names a mechanism, the post must explain that mechanism. No bait-and-switch.

## Flow Logic

- **How tension should build:** Open with the mistake or hidden cost (the viewer feels called out). Then explain the biological mechanism (the viewer feels educated). Then provide the fix (the viewer feels empowered).
- **How explanation should unfold:** Start with the familiar (a habit the viewer recognizes), then introduce the invisible mechanism (the biological process they did not know about), then connect it to performance (the outcome they care about).
- **How fixes or takeaways should land:** As specific, numbered actions with time ranges, dosages, or frequencies. "Do X for Y minutes at Z intensity" is more valuable than "Do X more."
- **How product mentions should be earned, if relevant:** The product must solve a problem that the post has already established as real and mechanistically explained. The mention should feel like a natural extension of the protocol, not an interruption.

## Editing Heuristics

- Cut any sentence that does not advance the mechanism, the fix, or the source.
- If a claim cannot be sourced, soften it or remove it.
- Replace "you should" with a direct mechanism + outcome ("When you do X, Y happens").
- Test every sentence against: "Would a competitive athlete who reads this feel smarter, or feel patronized?"
