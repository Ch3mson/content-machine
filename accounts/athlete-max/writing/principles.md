# Writing Principles

Use this file for stable account-specific principles extracted from competitor,
inspiration, and approved account examples.

## Source Summary

- **Primary reference accounts:** chasingpeaks0 (depth, narrative flow, accessible science), verified_byhumans (clean structure, clinical authority, source citations)
- **Strongest reusable lessons:** Problem-first hooks, desire-led benefit translation, mechanism-before-protocol flow, specific numbers/time ranges, source citations for trust, accessible analogies for complex biology
- **Weak patterns rejected:** Overly dense text blocks without scannability, generic listicles without mechanism, unsourced extreme claims, motivational framing

## Core Principles

| Principle | What It Means | Evidence | How To Use It |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Problem-first hook** | The first slide names a specific mistake, misconception, or invisible cost the viewer is already paying | Both chasingpeaks0 and verified_byhumans open by naming what the viewer is doing wrong | Lead with "You are probably doing X wrong" or "What to avoid:" rather than "Here are 3 tips" |
| **Desire concretion** | Each slide turns a hazy desire into a tangible outcome the reader can picture | verified_byhumans Post 2 turns public speaking into attractive conversations and salary negotiation, bouldering into forearms and confidence, and BJJ into stress immunity; Post 3 turns body-language errors into social authority fixes | Before drafting a slide, write the desired outcome in concrete terms. For athlete-max: first step to the through ball, cleaner touch after a sprint, sharper late-game read, roster trust, coach-visible stamina |
| **One avatar through the list** | A list post should feel like different answers to the same person's desire, not a bag of tips | verified_byhumans Post 2 and Post 3 keep one reader in mind across every slide, then reverse-engineer what that reader wants and likely does wrong | Define the reader's desire stack before choosing slide topics. Every slide must feel like it belongs to that same athlete |
| **Mechanism before protocol** | Explain the biological or neurological mechanism that makes the old way fail before listing the new way | chasingpeaks0 explains declarative vs. procedural memory before listing the fix; verified_byhumans explains HPA axis before giving the fix | Every "fix" slide must answer "Why does this work?" at a mechanistic level |
| **Specificity over generality** | Use exact numbers, time ranges, dosages, and percentages instead of vague qualifiers | "70-80% comprehension," "15-20 min/day," "30-60+ min/day," "60-90 minutes" | Replace "a lot" with a number. Replace "often" with a frequency. Replace "better" with a measurable outcome. |
| **Accessible analogies** | Translate complex sport/biology jargon into everyday comparisons the viewer already understands | "Procedural memory = the system that controls how you ride a bike," "phonological loop = working memory buffer" | When introducing a technical term, immediately parenthesize an analogy or simple definition |
| **Source citations for strong claims** | Cite journal name and PMID/PMCID for mechanistic or health claims that could be challenged | verified_byhumans cites "american journal of orthodontics and dentofacial orthopedics / nih (pmid: 3182177)" | Add a "source:" line at the bottom of any slide making a causal biological claim. If no source exists, soften the claim or remove it. |
| **Actionable binary** | Structure the core of the post as "what to avoid" vs. "the fix" so the viewer knows exactly what to stop and what to start | verified_byhumans uses "what to avoid:" → "the fix:" consistently | Every post should have a clear "stop doing this" and "start doing this instead" moment |
| **Identity aspiration without motivation** | Frame the outcome as becoming a smarter, more optimized athlete — not a "grind harder" warrior | "Like a PEAK Human," "Complete Optimization," "biological ROI" | Use aspirational framing that appeals to intelligence and efficiency, not willpower and suffering |
| **Performance translation** | Every biological mechanism must be tied to an in-competition, in-game, or in-practice outcome the competitive athlete cares about | "Slower reaction time," "worse fourth-quarter decisions," "missed reads when fatigued," "higher injury risk under load" | After explaining the mechanism, ask: "So what?" and answer with a specific performance cost or gain |
| **Line-fill writing** | Copy is written to the container, not free-form. Every paragraph must wrap so that each line fills roughly the same width — no short orphan lines, no ragged trailing words | verified_byhumans Post 2 slides 2-6: every text block forms a dense visual rectangle where lines run edge-to-edge at near-equal length | After drafting copy, test it against the font size and column width in the renderer. If a line fills less than 85% of the longest line in the same block, reword the sentence to redistribute characters evenly. This is a writing task, not a layout task — never fix line fill by changing font size or column width |

## Voice And Rhythm

- **Sentence length:** Mix short punchy statements (1-2 lines) with longer explanatory sentences (3-4 lines). No paragraph should exceed 5 lines on a 1080x1350 canvas.
- **Slide pacing:** 1 idea per slide. The hook slide is 1-2 lines. The problem slide is 3-5 bullet points. The mechanism slide is 2-3 short paragraphs. The fix slide is 3-5 numbered items. The source slide is 1 line.
- **Hook behavior:** The hook must name a private frustration the athlete already feels — in plain language, not jargon. The best hooks use curiosity gaps (quoted words, contrarian framing) or reframe a familiar failure as a solvable biological problem. If a 14-year-old athlete cannot instantly understand what the post is about and why they should care, the hook fails.
- **Explanation style:** Direct, second-person, desire-aware, and mechanism-focused. "When you do X, your brain/body does Y. That is why Z fails in the moment you care about."
- **CTA or close behavior:** Minimal. If a product mention is needed, it should be camouflaged as a source, tool, or "full protocol" reference. No hard sells.

## Authority And Trust

- **How the account earns trust:** By showing the biological mechanism behind every claim and citing verifiable sources. The voice is a "clinical coach who reads journals so you don't have to."
- **How claims should be framed:** Causal claims need sources. Correlational claims should be framed as "is associated with" or "research suggests." Avoid absolute language ("always," "never," "guaranteed") unless the biology is truly settled.
- **What makes the voice feel fake:** Motivational language, bro-science jargon without explanation, unsupported absolutes, aggressive "alpha" framing, credential bragging instead of source citing.
