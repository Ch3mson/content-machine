# Refinement Personas

Use this file before approving copy samples, running Workflow A3, or revising a
rejected quality-gate sample. Keep exactly three personas. Do not add extra
personas for this account; sharpen these three instead.

## Protocol Rules

- Each persona has one job.
- Each critique must separate blocking failures from non-blocking improvements.
- Each critique must produce at least one concrete rewrite action.
- Existing athlete-max docs can guide audience, voice, and format, but cannot
  prove sport terminology is native.
- One rejected sample creates a sample note. Repeated or explicit user feedback
  creates a candidate preference. Only validated durable feedback becomes a
  locked rule in `writing.md`, `writing/principles.md`, or `design.md`.
- Resolve persona conflicts in this order: claim safety and realism, instant
  value, then account/platform fit.
- The user's latest correction is binding: do not confuse mechanism explanation
  with desire fulfillment. Each slide must cement the athlete's hazy desired
  outcome into a tangible benefit.

## Persona 1: The Bored Athlete

### Invariant Job

Owns instant value, curiosity, and time-to-value.

### Account-Specific Calibration

- **Target athlete:** Competitive athletes across sports who want practical,
  science-backed performance gains without motivational fluff.
- **Primary desire:** Train smarter and see a performance edge in practice,
  games, trials, tournaments, or coach-visible moments.
- **Desire-concretion mandate:** Treat desire as hazy until the slide turns it
  into a specific outcome the athlete can picture: beat a defender to the ball,
  keep touch after repeat sprints, earn trust from a coach, recover confidence
  after pressure, or stop fading when selection decisions are visible.
- **Private frustration:** They train hard but still hesitate, fade, overthink,
  lose touch, make late reads, or fail to access skill under pressure.
- **What they already know:** Generic advice like sleep more, hydrate, work
  harder, and stay confident.
- **What they are tired of hearing:** Motivation, bro-science, jargon-heavy
  explanations, and vague "optimize yourself" claims.
- **Fastest believable payoff:** One mechanism-backed adjustment they can try
  this week and connect to a specific athletic moment.

### Value Equation Check

| Lever | athlete-max Standard |
| --- | --- |
| Dream outcome | The hook names a performance result the athlete wants: sharper reads, faster first step, cleaner late-game touch, better repeat sprints, calmer trial performance, or confidence that survives pressure. |
| Perceived likelihood | The slide gives a concrete mechanism, source direction, or observable before/after that makes the fix feel believable. |
| Time delay | The payoff feels close enough to test in the next practice, game, sleep cycle, warmup, or training week. |
| Effort cost | The action sounds bounded and doable, not like a full lifestyle overhaul. |

### Pass Criteria

- The first slide makes the performance value clear within 3 seconds.
- The hook creates curiosity without hiding the benefit.
- The copy makes the athlete think, "That is exactly what happens to me."
- The first payoff is concrete before the science gets dense.
- Each slide gives the athlete a reason to care before it asks them to learn.

### Blocking Failures

- The first slide teaches before making the athlete care.
- The value is abstract, delayed, or hard to picture.
- The slide explains the mechanism but never says what the athlete gets from it.
- The hook sounds like a lesson title instead of a performance problem.
- The copy asks for trust before showing why the athlete should keep reading.

### Non-Blocking Improvements

- The payoff is clear but could name a sharper sport moment.
- The curiosity gap works but could remove one generic word.

### Rewrite Actions

- Move the performance payoff into the first line.
- Replace abstract benefit language with a concrete game, practice, trial, or
  competition moment.
- Lower perceived effort by naming the smallest usable action.
- Convert mechanism-first wording into desire-first wording.
- Ask: what does this help the athlete win, avoid, feel, or become this week?

### Strong And Weak Examples

| Weak | Stronger |
| --- | --- |
| How sleep affects athletic performance | Why your first step feels late on game day |
| The science of fatigue | Why your legs feel heavy in the 4th quarter |
| Optimize your reaction time | Why you see the pass but react half a second late |
| Dehydration reduces cognitive performance | Why your first touch gets loose after repeat sprints |

## Persona 2: The Account-Native Scroller

### Invariant Job

Owns account fit, platform behavior, swipe momentum, and sentence flow.

### Account-Specific Calibration

- **Inspiration accounts/posts to compare against:** `chasingpeaks0` Post 1 and
  Post 2 for problem-to-science breakdowns; `verified_byhumans` Post 1 and Post
  2 for clean "what to avoid" / "the fix" flow, scannable labels, source
  citation structure, and instant reader benefit; `verified_byhumans` Post 3 for
  one-overarching-reader immersion, weak-behavior reverse engineering, and
  tangible desire extraction across every slide.
- **Hook shapes to preserve:** Direct private problem, contrarian correction,
  quoted familiar excuse, or familiar athletic failure reframed as a solvable
  biological problem.
- **Sentence rhythm:** Short hook, then compact explanatory sentences that build
  mechanism and consequence. Avoid long textbook paragraphs.
- **Line length:** Dense enough to feel substantive, but broken so a mobile
  reader can scan without losing the thread.
- **Pacing:** Problem first, mechanism second, fix third, source last. Do not
  jump into protocols before the reader understands why the old way fails.
- **Desire continuity:** A carousel should feel like it is talking to one athlete
  with one desire stack, not a list of useful facts for different people.
- **Density per slide:** One slide can carry detail, but each line needs a job:
  problem, mechanism, performance cost, fix, or source.
- **Visual reading constraints:** Follow `design.md`; do not use copy density to
  compensate for weak hierarchy or spacing.

### Reference-Flow Comparison

Compare the draft against athlete-max inspiration posts for:

- hook shape
- sentence rhythm
- line length
- pacing between short and explanatory sentences
- problem-to-mechanism-to-fix movement
- recurring reader desire and tangible benefit per slide
- density per slide
- smoothness and swipe momentum

### Pass Criteria

- The draft feels like a valid athlete-max descendant of chasingpeaks0 and
  verified_byhumans, not copied wording.
- Each slide gives the reader a reason to continue.
- Each slide makes the benefit instant, even if the mechanism is dense.
- The copy sounds smooth when read aloud.
- The structure feels clinical, useful, and native to TikTok/Instagram.

### Blocking Failures

- The post could fit any generic sports, fitness, science, or motivation page.
- The copy feels clunky, over-explained, or detached from the reference flow.
- The slide density fights the account design system.
- The post becomes a random list instead of one connected athlete desire stack.
- The copy borrows verified_byhumans structure but loses its desire extraction.

### Non-Blocking Improvements

- The copy is native but could use a sharper section label.
- A sentence is accurate but can be split for smoother swipe momentum.

### Rewrite Actions

- Match the closest reference flow: problem, mechanism, fix, source.
- Cut or split sentences that break swipe momentum.
- Reorder slides so each one answers the question raised by the previous slide.
- Before approving a list slide, name the one reader it belongs to and the exact
  benefit that reader gets from that slide.
- Replace generic labels with account-native labels like "what to avoid," "the
  fix," "why it works," or "performance cost" when the flow calls for them.

### Strong And Weak Examples

| Weak | Stronger |
| --- | --- |
| Here are three recovery tips for athletes | what to avoid: treating recovery like rest |
| This post explains hydration science | why you process slower before you feel thirsty |
| Improve your performance with better habits | the small habit that makes your fourth-quarter reads late |
| 3 recovery mistakes athletes make | 3 habits that make your late-game touch disappear |

## Persona 3: The Athlete Skeptic

### Invariant Job

Owns realism, terminology, claim safety, and w(inner) camouflage.

### Account-Specific Calibration

- **Sports or athlete contexts covered:** Broad competitive athletes across
  team, individual, endurance, court, field, and combat sports.
- **Audience level:** Serious youth, university, aspiring elite, and competitive
  recreational athletes who can understand science if it is translated.
- **Terms likely to be too niche:** Highly sport-specific coaching terms without
  context, lab-only physiology terms, and acronyms that do not affect the
  immediate performance point.
- **Terms likely to be fake or AI-generated:** "pressure-state optimization,"
  "competitive identity activation," "neuro-athletic unlock," "performance
  mindset reprogramming," "clutch-state protocol," and other phrases that sound
  polished but not coach-native.
- **Claim types that require sourcing:** Causal biological claims, percentages,
  sleep effects, hydration and cognition claims, recovery windows, supplement
  effects, injury-adjacent claims, and any claim that implies mental performance
  improvement.
- **Product-mention risk:** w(inner) must feel like a natural mental-rep or
  visualization tool. It cannot sound like therapy, a meditation app, a
  guaranteed performance fix, or a sales pitch.

### Flagged-Term Protocol

Only research flagged terms, not every phrase. Flag a term when it is:

- sport-specific
- technical
- abstract
- unusually polished
- likely AI-generated
- unfamiliar to a teammate or coach
- central to the slide's credibility

### Verification Ladder

1. **Coach/teammate test**
   - Would a real teammate or coach plausibly say this out loud?
   - If no, rewrite into observable performance language.
2. **External research check**
   - Search the exact phrase with the sport context.
   - Prefer coaching resources, athlete interviews, official sport education,
     credible training material, reputable sport-science sources, and real
     athlete communities.
   - Do not rely on SEO blogs or generic AI-like articles as proof.
3. **Confidence tier**
   - `Native`: clear evidence of real athlete, coach, or sport-community usage.
   - `Acceptable`: plain and understandable even if not a specialized term.
   - `Too niche`: real term but wrong for this broad account audience.
   - `AI jargon`: sounds technical but lacks real sport usage.
   - `Unverified`: cannot confirm, so rewrite simpler.

Default action: if a term is not clearly `Native` or `Acceptable`, rewrite it
into plain, observable athlete language.

### Verb-Adjective Combination Test

Even when individual words are common, flag phrases that sound grammatically
correct but not locker-room native. Examples:
- "drops cleanly" → "stays clean"
- "executes precisely" → "hits clean"
- "maintains optimal output" → "keeps sharp"
- "dialing precision down" → "suppresses fine motor output"

Test: would a teammate say this after practice? If no, rewrite.

### Pass Criteria

- Sport language sounds like something a teammate or coach would recognize.
- Technical terms are translated into plain performance consequences.
- Tangible benefits stay realistic and do not become exaggerated guarantees.
- Strong claims are sourced, softened, or removed.
- w(inner) mentions feel like a natural training tool, source, or protocol
  extension, not an ad.

### Blocking Failures

- AI-generated sport jargon.
- Unsupported causal claims.
- Guaranteed performance, therapy, medical, scholarship, contract, or victory
  claims.
- Desire language that promises status, selection, or game outcomes the evidence
  cannot support.
- Product mentions that sound like sales copy.
- Sport-specific terms that are too niche for a broad athlete account and not
  explained.

### Non-Blocking Improvements

- A term is acceptable but could be made more coach-native.
- A source is likely needed before the post becomes final, but the sample can
  proceed if the claim is marked for verification.

### Rewrite Actions

- Replace suspect terms with observable athlete language.
- Add a source or soften the claim.
- Remove or camouflage product language until it passes `product/claim-bank.md`.
- Translate technical mechanisms into a coach-visible or teammate-visible
  performance effect.
- Keep the benefit concrete without implying the protocol guarantees a goal,
  roster spot, win, contract, or medical result.
- When a mechanism action is named, explain the plain purpose in the same
  sentence or the very next line. Never leave "why?" unanswered.
- Causal sport language (e.g., "leg problem," "tired legs") must pass the
  locker-room plausibility test. If a teammate would say "conditioning" instead
  of "legs," use the teammate version.

### Strong And Weak Examples

| Weak | Stronger |
| --- | --- |
| pressure-state optimization | your first touch gets worse when the game speeds up |
| competitive identity activation | you stop playing like the version of yourself from practice |
| neuro-athletic unlock | your brain recognizes the read before your body reacts |
| clutch-state protocol | a pre-game routine for the moments you usually rush |
| hydration makes you dominate late games | hydration keeps your touch from getting loose after repeat sprints |
