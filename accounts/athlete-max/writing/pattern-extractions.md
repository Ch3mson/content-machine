## Viewer-Perspective Notes

### chasingpeaks0 Post 1

- **What makes the viewer stop?** The promise of learning "like a PEAK Human" combined with "According to neuroscience" signals that this is not generic advice.
- **What private frustration does it name?** Most people study languages for years with poor results. The post names 4 specific mistakes (grammar memorization, vocab lists, waiting to speak, long sessions) that feel familiar and embarrassing.
- **What promise keeps the viewer swiping?** The implicit promise: "Your brain is not broken; your method is. Here is the actual mechanism and the fix."
- **Where does trust come from?** The explanation of declarative vs. procedural memory and the phonological loop shows real scientific literacy, not buzzword dropping.
- **What feels generic or weak?** The final CTA slide ("link in bio") is functional but not distinctive. The "PEAK Human" framing could feel overused if repeated too often.

- **What private frustration does it name?** The feeling that morning/night routines are either too basic ("drink water") or too complex (biohacking stacks). This splits the difference with 4 evidence-backed items.

## Candidate Patterns

- **Problem-first hook:** Name the mistake or misconception before promising the fix.
- **Mechanism-before-protocol:** Explain why the old way fails at a biological level before listing what to do instead.
- **Specific numbers and time ranges:** "70-80% comprehension," "15-20 min/day," "60-90 minutes" — these signal research-backed precision.
- **Source citations:** Journal name + NIH/PMID at the bottom of slides that make strong mechanistic claims.
- **"What to avoid" → "The fix" structure:** Clear binary that makes the viewer feel smart for avoiding the mistake.
- **Accessible analogies for complex mechanisms:** "Procedural memory = riding a bike," "phonological loop = working memory buffer."
- **Aspirational identity framing:** "Like a PEAK Human," "Complete Optimization" — these let the viewer imagine a better version of themselves without motivational language.

## Rejected Patterns

- **Overly dense text blocks:** chasingpeaks0 slides 3-4 are text-heavy; verified_byhumans shows that clean white space + selective bolding improves scannability.
- **Generic listicle without mechanism:** "5 tips to recover faster" without explaining the biological mechanism would feel like bro-science.
- **Part-series dependency:** "Part 1" creates engagement but risks disappointment if the viewer cannot find Part 2. athlete-max posts should be self-contained unless intentionally serialized.
- **Unsourced extreme claims:** "permanent neurological damage" or "completely hiding your jawline" — these work for shock value but need bulletproof sourcing or softer framing for a science-first account.
