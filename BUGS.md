## 2026-04-28 — Quality-gate copy missed desire extraction

- What broke: The athlete-max writing system and first quality-gate sample explained mechanisms and protocols, but did not make the reader benefit instant and concrete on each slide.
- Root cause: The extraction captured `mechanism before protocol` and `performance translation`, but missed the verified_byhumans pattern of starting from one overarching reader desire and tying every slide to a tangible wanted outcome.
- Fix: Add desire concretion / instant benefit rules to `writing.md` and `writing/principles.md`, then revise the quality-gate sample so a soccer forward instantly sees the payoff.
- Pattern to avoid: Do not treat mechanism explanation as the benefit. Every slide needs a specific reader-facing payoff: what they get, avoid, feel, win, or become.

## 2026-04-28 — Sample posts used jargon and buried instant value

- What broke: The athlete-max sample posts used technical terms ("suprachiasmatic nucleus," "ventilatory threshold," "adenosine," "prefrontal cortex") in the body copy, and front-loaded mechanism explanations before cementing the performance payoff on each slide.
- Root cause: The Athlete Skeptic persona's default action (rewrite non-Native terms into plain language) was not applied during drafting. The Bored Athlete's mandate (concrete payoff before dense science, value clear in 2 seconds) was violated by explaining mechanisms before showing what the athlete gets.
- Fix: Rewrote both approved samples to pass all three refinement personas. Removed all non-Native technical terms from hooks and early slides. Restructured every slide to lead with the performance consequence, then explain the mechanism.
- Pattern to avoid: Do not use technical terms in the first half of a post unless they are clearly Native (something a teammate would say in casual conversation). Never front-load a mechanism before the athlete understands what they get from it. If a term fails the locker-room test, rewrite it into the performance consequence.

## 2026-05-01 — Athlete-max hook used vague metaphor instead of precise performance language

- What broke: A loss-aversion hook used the phrase "play smaller," which sounded vague and weak for athlete-max copy.
- Root cause: The hook leaned on a generic metaphor instead of naming the exact performance consequence an athlete would recognize.
- Fix: Replace vague phrasing like "play smaller" with stronger, accurate verbs and concrete outcomes, such as "lose confidence," "hesitate," "second-guess reads," "avoid contact," or "stop attacking space."
- Pattern to avoid: For athlete-max, do not use broad metaphorical performance language when a precise observable action or feeling exists. Name what changes in the athlete's body, decision, confidence, timing, or execution.

## 2026-05-01 — Athlete-max draft used unnatural terminology and impractical reset actions

- What broke: A pressure-response draft used uncommon words like "cascade" and suggested tapping the sternum as a reset cue, which felt unnatural and impractical for real athletes.
- Root cause: The copy prioritized scientific-sounding structure over locker-room-native language and realistic in-game behavior.
- Fix: Replace uncommon terms with simple performance language like "spiral," "loop," "reaction," or "pattern." Use practical actions athletes already do, such as one long exhale, look up, scan the field/court, call one assignment, reset posture, or get back to position.
- Pattern to avoid: Do not invent reset rituals or cue phrases that athletes would not actually use in competition. Phrases like "find your matchup" sound written from outside the sport. Every protocol must pass the practical test: would a real athlete or coach say/do this between plays without feeling weird?
