## Reusable Principle

The deeper creative mechanism that could transfer to another niche.

Write this as an abstract pattern, not as source-specific wording.

- Principle name: Failure confession with warning system
- Principle summary: Lead with a personal collapse, then convert each mistake into a clear avoidable rule for the viewer. The post earns attention through regret, then keeps it by turning lived damage into a checklist.
- Safe rewrite formula: I became X by doing Y, Z, and W. If you want to avoid the same outcome, watch for these exact failure patterns.

## Psychological Mechanism

What makes the viewer care, pause, believe, or keep swiping?

- Mechanism: Self-incrimination plus prevention value
- Why it works: The creator lowers defensiveness by admitting fault, then raises relevance by implying the viewer could be next unless they learn the pattern.
- Viewer belief being challenged: Bad outcomes happen to other people, not me.
- Viewer desire or insecurity being activated: Fear of wasting potential, fear of self-sabotage, desire for a shortcut through someone else’s expensive lesson.

## Hook Archetype

- Archetype: failure-confession
- First-frame promise: This is exactly how I ruined my own path, and how you can avoid it.
- Hook engine: warning
- What must not be copied verbatim: The literal D1 wording, the team/offer specifics, and the exact slide phrasing.

## Emotional Lever

Select and explain the dominant levers.

- Fear: High; the post frames missed potential as a preventable self-inflicted loss.
- Status: High; D1 access functions as elite-status loss.
- Shame: High; the narrator exposes his own bad decisions.
- Curiosity: Moderate; viewers want the full list of mistakes.
- Aspiration: Moderate; the warning implies a better path is still available.
- Identity: High; viewers who see themselves as ambitious athletes feel targeted.
- Urgency: Moderate; the lesson is framed as time-sensitive because habits compound.
- Relief: Moderate; the post offers a map to avoid the same outcome.
- Other: Regret-to-rule conversion.

## Curiosity Gap

What question does the viewer need answered?

- Open loop: What did he do that turned a real opportunity into a bust?
- Why the viewer keeps swiping/watching: Each slide feels like another costly mistake they can learn from.
- Payoff promised: A concrete list of behaviors that destroy athletic trajectories.

## Proof Pattern

What makes the claim feel believable?

- Proof type: personal story
- Specific proof moments: Signed with a major program, then describes complacency, distraction, ignoring details, impatience, and poor discipline as the path to decline.
- Claim-risk notes: The exact career outcome is strong proof but should be used carefully if adapting to another account; don’t overstate causal certainty without support.
- What would need verification before using this in our account: Any performance claims, recruiting details, or causal assertions beyond the creator’s testimony.


## Visual Pattern

How do visuals create attention, proof, trust, or emotion?

- First-slide visual move: Large action football image with bold red title blocks centered low on the frame.
- Body-slide layout pattern: Full-screen athlete photos with white text boxes placed to avoid covering faces and key action.
- Text hierarchy: Short numbered headline, then a compact explanatory paragraph, then a takeaway line.
- Image style: Natural sports photography, mostly candid action and sideline portraits.
- Color / contrast strategy: High-contrast red and white on busy football imagery; strong legibility through boxed text.
- Evidence cues: Player identity, locker-room/field context, team gear, and consistent same-subject imagery.
- What is reusable: Numbered mistake sequence, one-sentence lesson headers, and clear text boxes over authentic action photos.
- What is source-specific: The football recruiting context, school logos, and the exact jersey/team imagery.
